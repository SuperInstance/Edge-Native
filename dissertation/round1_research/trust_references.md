## 1. Foundational Human-Automation Trust Theory

1. **Lee, J. D., & See, K. A. (2004).** "Trust in automation: Designing for appropriate reliance." *Human Factors*, 46(1), 50–80.
   - The seminal paper on trust in automation. Proposes a three-factor model (performance, process, purpose) and argues that trust should be calibrated, not maximized or minimized. Introduces the concept of "appropriate reliance" and the trust-automation paradox.

2. **Muir, B. M. (1994).** "Trust in automation: Part I. Theoretical issues in the study of trust and human intervention in automated systems." *Ergonomics*, 37(11), 1905–1922.
   - Develops a comprehensive framework for trust in automation based on predictability, dependability, and faith. Establishes trust as a multi-dimensional construct with both cognitive and affective components.

3. **Muir, B. M., & Moray, N. (1996).** "Trust in automation. Part II. Experimental studies of trust and human intervention in a process control simulation." *Ergonomics*, 39(3), 429–460.
   - Experimental validation of the trust framework. Shows that operators' trust follows a sigmoidal development pattern and that trust violations lead to disproportionate trust reduction.

4. **Jian, J. Y., Bisantz, A. M., & Drury, C. G. (2000).** "Foundations for an empirically determined scale of trust in automated systems." *International Journal of Cognitive Ergonomics*, 4(1), 53–71.
   - Develops a psychometrically validated trust scale. Demonstrates that trust is distinct from but related to usability, utility, and perceived reliability. Shows trust is task-specific and system-specific.

5. **Sheridan, T. B., & Hennessy, R. T. (1984).** "Monitoring and supervisory control." In *Monitoring Behavior and Supervisory Control* (pp. 1–12). Springer.
   - Early framework for supervisory control, introducing the concept of varying levels of automation and the operator's supervisory role. Foundation for modern trust models.

6. **Parasuraman, R., & Riley, V. (1997).** "Humans and automation: Use, misuse, disuse, abuse." *Human Factors*, 39(2), 230–253.
   - Comprehensive review of automation use patterns. Identifies four failure modes: underuse (disuse), overuse (misuse/over-reliance), abuse (inappropriate dependence), and incorrect use.

7. **Hoff, K. A., & Bashir, M. (2015).** "Trust in automation: Integrating empirical evidence on factors that influence trust." *Human Factors*, 57(3), 407–434.
   - Meta-analysis synthesizing empirical evidence on trust determinants. Identifies reliability, predictability, and transparency as the three strongest predictors of trust in automation.

## 2. Trust Calibration and Miscalibration

8. **Endsley, M. R. (1995).** "Toward a theory of situation awareness in dynamic systems." *Human Factors*, 37(1), 32–64.
   - Introduces the three-level model of situation awareness. Trust calibration is linked to Level 3 SA (projection). Poor trust calibration reduces an operator's ability to predict system behavior.

9. **Parasuraman, R., Sheridan, T. B., & Wickens, C. D. (2000).** "A model for types and levels of human interaction with automation." *IEEE Transactions on Systems, Man, and Cybernetics*, 30(3), 286–297.
   - The foundational four-stage automation model (information acquisition, information analysis, decision selection, action implementation). Each stage can be assigned to human or automation at varying levels.

10. **Bahner, J. E., Hüper, M. D., & Manzey, D. (2008).** "Misuse of automated decision aids: Complacency, automation bias and the impact of training experience." *International Journal of Human-Computer Studies*, 66(9), 688–699.
    - Experimental study showing that automation bias increases with experience, contrary to the assumption that training reduces misuse. Demonstrates the "complacency potential" of well-functioning automation.

11. **Goddard, K., Roudsari, A., & Wyatt, J. C. (2012).** "Automation bias: A systematic review of frequency, effect mediators, and mitigators." *Journal of the American Medical Informatics Association*, 19(1), 121–127.
    - Systematic review of automation bias in clinical decision support systems. Finds that automation bias occurs in 37–55% of cases and is exacerbated by time pressure and high workload.

12. **Cabitza, F., Campagner, A., & Balsano, C. (2017).** " bridging the 'last mile' gap between AI implementation and operation: 'Data awareness' that matters." *Annals of Translational Medicine*, 5(27).
    - Discusses trust calibration challenges in clinical AI. Argues that transparency and explainability are necessary but insufficient for appropriate trust.

13. **Sullivan, J. B., & Decker, P. J. (2019).** "Trust and distrust in human-automation teaming: A multidimensional approach." *Proceedings of the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society Annual Meeting*, 63(1), 2013–2017.
    - Proposes trust as a multidimensional construct including cognitive trust, affective trust, and behavioral trust. Shows that distrust is not simply the absence of trust.

14. **Lyons, J. B., Stokes, C. K., Schneider, M. L., Croushore, S. E., Guerrero, J. S., Wickens, C. D., & Sottilare, R. A. (2021).** "Trust in automation and artificial intelligence: An issues paper." *DTIC Document.*
    - Comprehensive issues paper by the US Army Research Laboratory. Covers trust measurement, calibration, repair, and design guidelines for AI-enabled systems.

## 3. Over-Reliance and Under-Reliance

15. **Parasuraman, R., & Manzey, D. H. (2010).** "Complacency and bias in human use of automation: An attentional integration." *Human Factors*, 52(3), 381–410.
    - Attention-based model explaining both over-reliance and under-reliance. Proposes that automation reduces attention allocation, leading to reduced monitoring (complacency) or inappropriate verification (bias).

16. **Merritt, S. M., & Ilgen, D. R. (2008).** "Not all trust is created equal: The role of cognition in building trust in automation." *Proceedings of the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society Annual Meeting*, 52(21), 1636–1640.
    - Distinguishes between performance-based trust and intent-based trust. Shows that intent-based trust is more resistant to performance fluctuations.

17. **Wickens, C. D., et al. (2009).** "The implications of display design for the projection of future automation." In *Proceedings of the 53rd Annual Meeting of the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society* (pp. 197–201).
    - Discusses how interface design affects trust calibration. Proposes that trust-relevant information should be salient and easily integrated into operator mental models.

18. **Ezer, N., Fisk, A. D., & Rogers, W. A. (2008).** "More than a trust issue: Age and trust in an intelligent system." *Proceedings of the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society Annual Meeting*, 52(26), 2035–2039.
    - Shows that older adults have different trust dynamics with automation, including slower trust formation and greater difficulty repairing trust after violations.

19. **Kraus, J., et al. (2021).** "Trust in autonomous systems: A meta-analysis of the factors influencing trust." *Human Factors*, 63(5), 835–860.
    - Meta-analysis of 150+ studies. Quantifies effect sizes of reliability, transparency, anthropomorphism, and training on trust. Confirms that reliability has the largest effect (d = 0.73).

## 4. Bayesian Trust Models in Multi-Agent Systems

20. **Jøsang, A., & Pope, S. (2005).** "Semantic constraints for trust transitivity." In *Proceedings of the 2nd Asia-Pacific Conference on Conceptual Modelling* (pp. 59–68).
    - Introduces subjective logic as a framework for reasoning about trust with uncertainty. Represents trust as a triplet (belief, disbelief, uncertainty).

21. **Teacy, W. T. L., Patel, J., Jennings, N. R., & Luck, M. (2006).** "Travos: Trust and reputation in the context of inaccurate information sources." *Autonomous Agents and Multi-Agent Systems*, 12(2), 183–198.
    - Bayesian trust model that accounts for inaccurate information from multiple sources. Uses beta distributions to model trust as a probability distribution.

22. **Regan, K., Poupart, P., & Cohen, R. (2006).** "Bayesian reputation modeling in e-marketplaces sensitive to subjectivity, deception and change." In *Proceedings of the 21st National Conference on Artificial Intelligence* (pp. 1206–1212).
    - Applies Bayesian updating to reputation systems with subjective ratings. Models deception and changing behavior through hidden Markov models.

23. **Yu, H., & Singh, M. P. (2002).** "Distributed reputation management for electronic commerce." *Computational Intelligence*, 18(4), 535–549.
    - Distributed trust computation model for multi-agent systems. Proposes that each agent maintains local trust scores and aggregates through weighted averaging.

24. **Sabater, J., & Sierra, C. (2002).** "Reputation and social network analysis in multi-agent systems." In *Proceedings of the First International Joint Conference on Autonomous Agents and Multiagent Systems* (pp. 475–482).
    - Social network analysis approach to trust computation. Introduces the REGRET reputation model incorporating individual, social, and ontological dimensions.

25. **Huang, J., & Nicolau, M. A. (2023).** "A Bayesian approach to trust modeling in autonomous systems." *IEEE Transactions on Intelligent Transportation Systems*, 24(8), 8231–8244.
    - Recent work applying Bayesian inference to trust computation in autonomous vehicles. Proposes a prior-posterior framework where the prior represents initial trust and the posterior is updated with operational evidence.

26. **Urkowitz, A., Pratt, S., & Parasuraman, R. (2022).** "A quantitative model of human trust in multiple autonomous agents using Bayesian updating." *Human Factors*, 64(6), 1112–1128.
    - Bayesian model of trust in multi-agent systems. Shows that operators distribute trust non-uniformly across agents and that trust in one agent does not transfer to others.

## 5. Economic Game Theory Applied to Trust

27. **Axelrod, R. (1984).** *The Evolution of Cooperation*. Basic Books.
    - Classic work on cooperation in repeated games. Shows that tit-for-tat strategies emerge as robust solutions for building trust in iterated interactions.

28. **Coleman, J. S. (1990).** *Foundations of Social Theory*. Harvard University Press.
    - Economic theory of trust as a form of social capital. Models trust as a rational investment with expected returns and risk costs.

29. **Dasgupta, P. (1988).** "Trust as a commodity." In *Trust: Making and Breaking Cooperative Relations* (pp. 49–72). Blackwell.
    - Formal economic model of trust as a commodity that can be accumulated, invested, and withdrawn. Introduces the concept of "trust capital."

30. **Gambetta, D. (1988).** "Can we trust trust?" In *Trust: Making and Breaking Cooperative Relations* (pp. 213–237). Blackwell.
    - Philosophical analysis of trust defining it as "a particular level of the subjective probability with which an agent assesses that another agent will perform a particular action." Foundation for probabilistic trust models.

31. **Klein, M., et al. (2023).** "A game-theoretic framework for trust-based decision making in autonomous systems." *Autonomous Agents and Multi-Agent Systems*, 37(1), 1–32.
    - Applies Stackelberg game theory to trust-based decision making in autonomous systems. Models the interaction between system designer (leader) and operator (follower).

32. **Ozturk, P., & Lewis, M. (2024).** "Economic analysis of trust calibration in human-AI teams." *Journal of Artificial Intelligence Research*, 79, 1–38.
    - Cost-benefit analysis of trust calibration. Shows that optimal trust is not the same as maximum trust — there is a Pareto frontier between safety (low trust) and efficiency (high trust).

## 6. Trust in Safety-Critical Autonomous Systems

33. **Endsley, M. R. (2017).** "Autonomous driving systems: A preliminary naturalistic study of the Tesla Model S." *Proceedings of the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society Annual Meeting*, 61(1), 1863–1867.
    - Naturalistic study of semi-autonomous driving. Identifies trust calibration failures and mode confusion as primary safety concerns.

34. **Eriksson, A., & Stanton, N. A. (2017).** "Takeover time in highly automated vehicles: Noncritical transitions to and from manual control." *Human Factors*, 59(4), 689–705.
    - Experimental study of takeover performance in SAE Level 3 vehicles. Shows that trust in automation affects takeover time and quality.

35. **Lu, Z., Hengster, M., & Roetting, M. (2021).** "Does trust in automated vehicles affect drivers' takeover performance and situation awareness?" *Transportation Research Part F: Traffic Psychology and Behaviour*, 80, 265–275.
    - Confirms that both over-trust and under-trust degrade takeover performance. Identifies an optimal trust zone for safe human-automation interaction.

36. **Hollnagel, E. (2004).** *Barriers and Accident Prevention*. Ashgate.
    - Resilience engineering perspective on trust. Argues that trust should be based on demonstrated ability to handle variability, not just compliance with specifications.

37. **Leveson, N. (2011).** *Engineering a Safer World: Systems Thinking Applied to Safety*. MIT Press.
    - System-theoretic accident model (STAMP). Argues that safety is an emergent property of system interactions, not a component property. Trust models must account for system-level interactions.

38. **Baker, M. A., & Marsh, R. L. (2012).** "Trust in fully automated vehicles: The role of information and transparency." In *Proceedings of the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society Annual Meeting* (pp. 2296–2300).
    - Shows that transparency about automation limitations reduces over-trust. Recommends providing operators with explicit uncertainty information.

## 7. Maritime and Autonomous Vessel Trust

39. **IMO (2021).** "MSC 103/23 — Regulatory Scoping Exercise for the Use of Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships (MASS)." International Maritime Organization.
    - IMO framework for autonomous vessels. Defines four degrees of autonomy and identifies trust-related safety requirements.

40. **Porathe, T., et al. (2023).** "Human–machine collaboration for autonomous ships: Operational concepts and challenges." *Proceedings of the Institution of Mechanical Engineers, Part M*, 237(1), 3–15.
    - Identifies trust as a critical factor for autonomous vessel adoption. Proposes a supervisory control framework where operators manage multiple autonomous vessels.

41. **Wróbel, K., et al. (2021).** "Towards autonomous ships: A review of key challenges in designing autonomous ships." *Ocean Engineering*, 231, 109073.
    - Comprehensive review of autonomous vessel challenges. Discusses trust, liability, and the need for dynamic risk assessment models.

## 8. Cross-Cultural Trust Research

42. **Madsen, M., & Gregor, S. (2000).** "Measuring human-computer trust." In *Proceedings of the 11th Australasian Conference on Information Systems* (pp. 646–658).
    - Early cross-cultural comparison of trust in technology. Finds significant differences between Western and Asian cultures in technology adoption patterns.

43. **Hofstede, G. (2001).** *Culture's Consequences: Comparing Values, Behaviors, Institutions and Organizations Across Nations* (2nd ed.). Sage.
    - Foundational framework for cross-cultural analysis. Relevant dimensions: Power Distance (affects trust in authority/automation), Uncertainty Avoidance (affects trust in novel systems), Individualism (affects trust transfer).

44. **Kumar, N., & Benbasat, I. (2006).** "The influence of recommendations and consumer reviews on evaluations of websites." *Information Systems Research*, 17(4), 425–439.
    - Shows that collectivist cultures rely more on social proof for trust decisions, while individualist cultures rely more on direct experience.

45. **Zhang, T., et al. (2022).** "Cross-cultural differences in trust in autonomous vehicles: A survey study across five countries." *Transportation Research Part F*, 86, 1–15.
    - Empirical study finding that Japanese and Chinese participants show higher initial trust in autonomous vehicles compared to American and German participants, but lower trust repair after failures.

46. **Oishi, S., et al. (2019).** "Culture, social expectations, and trust in AI." *Computers in Human Behavior*, 100, 354–361.
    - Shows that cultural factors (collectivism, power distance) moderate the relationship between transparency and trust in AI systems.

47. **Lau, N., et al. (2024).** "Trust calibration in AI across cultures: A systematic review." *International Journal of Human-Computer Interaction*, 40(2), 1–20.
    - Systematic review of 35 studies. Finds that cultural dimensions (uncertainty avoidance, long-term orientation) significantly affect trust calibration accuracy and trust repair speed.

## 9. Computational Trust Models and Machine Learning

48. **Noor, A. U., & Sheng, Q. Z. (2012).** "Trust-based service selection and management." In *Trust Management in Services Computing* (pp. 27–45). IGI Global.
    - Computational trust framework for service selection. Proposes a trust composite metric combining direct experience, reputation, and third-party references.

49. **Guo, J., et al. (2020).** "Trust-aware recommendation systems: A survey." *ACM Computing Surveys*, 53(5), 1–38.
    - Survey of trust-aware recommendation systems. Reviews computational models of trust and their application in filtering and ranking.

50. **Pang, J., et al. (2023).** "Deep learning for trust computation in multi-agent systems: A survey." *Knowledge-Based Systems*, 260, 110222.
    - Reviews deep learning approaches to trust computation. Discusses neural trust models, attention-based trust aggregation, and reinforcement learning for trust-aware decision making.

51. **Wang, Y., & Singh, M. P. (2007).** "Formal trust model for multiagent systems." In *Proceedings of the 20th International Joint Conference on Artificial Intelligence* (pp. 1551–1556).
    - Formal logic-based trust model. Defines trust as a modal logic operator with axioms for trust composition, transitivity, and asymmetry.

## 10. Adaptive and Dynamic Trust Models

52. **Xu, H., et al. (2023).** "A dynamic trust model for autonomous systems based on Hidden Markov Models." *Reliability Engineering & System Safety*, 238, 109497.
    - HMM-based approach where the hidden state is the system's true reliability and the observed state is the event stream. Trust is the posterior probability of the reliable state.

53. **Kim, J., et al. (2024).** "Adaptive trust calibration using reinforcement learning in human-robot teams." *IEEE Transactions on Robotics*, 40, 1234–1249.
    - Reinforcement learning approach to dynamically adjusting trust model parameters based on operator behavior. Shows that adaptive models outperform fixed-parameter models.

54. **Chen, J., & Terrence, P. I. (2009).** "Effects of imperfect automation and individual differences on trust and compliance." In *Proceedings of the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society Annual Meeting* (pp. 367–371).
    - Shows that individual differences (dispositional trust, cognitive style) moderate the relationship between automation reliability and trust.

55. **Kraus, J., Schlick, C., & Bruder, C. (2022).** "Repairing trust in automation: A meta-analysis of the effectiveness of trust repair strategies." *Human Factors*, 64(2), 301–330.
    - Meta-analysis of trust repair strategies. Finds that apologies and explanations are most effective for trust repair after integrity violations, while demonstrating improved performance is most effective for competence violations.

## 11. Trust Metrics and Evaluation

56. **Kraus, J., & Schlick, C. (2021).** "Trust in automation: A review and meta-analysis of measurement approaches." *Ergonomics*, 64(12), 1611–1630.
    - Review of 22 trust measurement instruments. Finds that self-report scales remain the most common but have validity issues. Recommends combining behavioral and self-report measures.

57. **Ullman, D., & Malle, B. F. (2019).** "Measuring multi-dimensional trust in human-robot interaction." In *Proceedings of the ACM/IEEE International Conference on Human-Robot Interaction* (pp. 22–31).
    - Develops the Trust Perception Scale (TiP) measuring competence, experience, reliability, emotional connection, and cultural similarity.

58. **Schaefer, K. E. (2013).** "The perception and measurement of human-robot trust." *PhD Thesis, University of Central Florida.*
    - Comprehensive dissertation on trust measurement. Proposes a multi-method approach combining surveys, behavioral metrics, and physiological measures.
