1. HYDRAULIC STEERING SYSTEM TYPES
================================================================================

1.1  OVERVIEW
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Hydraulic steering replaces mechanical push-pull cable systems with hydraulic
fluid pressure to actuate the rudder. The autopilot controls the hydraulic
flow by energizing solenoid valves that direct pressurized fluid to one side
or the other of a hydraulic cylinder, which in turn pushes or pulls the
rudder stock via a tiller arm or quadrant.

The autopilot does NOT create hydraulic pressure itself. A dedicated
hydraulic pump (engine-driven, electric, or manual) maintains system
pressure. The autopilot's solenoid valves CONTROL the direction of flow.

1.2  SINGLE-CYLINDER HYDRAULIC RAM
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Description  : One double-acting hydraulic cylinder mounted athwartships
               (side to side) near the rudder stock.
Vessel Size  : Most common on sailboats 25–45 ft LOA.
Rudder Auth. : Adequate for vessels with balanced or semi-balanced
               spade rudders. Typical ram stroke: 6–10 inches (150–250 mm).
Cylinder Bore: 1.0–1.5 inches (25–38 mm) typical.

Operation    :
  - Fluid under pressure (from pump/reservoir) is always available at the
    solenoid valve block.
  - When PORT solenoid energizes → fluid flows to the port side of the
    cylinder, starboard side vents to reservoir → rudder moves to PORT.
  - When STARBOARD solenoid energizes → fluid flows to starboard side →
    rudder moves to STARBOARD.
  - Both de-energized → spring-centered valve blocks flow → rudder holds
    position (hydraulic lock).

Stroke-to-Angle Relationship:
  For a tiller arm of length L (measured from rudder stock center to
  cylinder pin center):
    Angle (degrees) = arctan(stroke / (2 × L)) × (180/π)
  Example: L = 100 mm, stroke = 150 mm
    Max angle = arctan(150/200) = 36.87 degrees (each side)
  This yields approximately ±37 degrees of rudder throw.

1.3  DUAL-CYLINDER SYSTEM
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Description  : Two hydraulic cylinders, one on each side of the rudder stock,
               operating in push-pull configuration.
Vessel Size  : Larger sailboats 45–65 ft and motor vessels requiring high
               rudder authority (high displacement, large rudder area).
Rudder Auth. : Significantly higher — dual cylinders can deliver nearly
               double the force of a single cylinder system. Essential for
               vessels operating in heavy weather or with high sail plans.
Cylinder Bore: 1.5–2.0 inches (38–50 mm) typical per cylinder.

Advantages   :
  - Redundancy: system can often operate (reduced) on one cylinder
  - Balanced loading on rudder stock
  - Higher total force output
Disadvantages :
  - More complex plumbing
  - Higher fluid volume = slower response (larger pump needed)
  - More expensive

Autopilot Control:
  - Single solenoid valve block drives both cylinders simultaneously
  - Same control logic as single-cylinder
  - Higher current draw may require heavier wiring and higher-rated MOSFETs

1.4  LINEAR HYDRAULIC DRIVE (CABLE REPLACEMENT)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Description  : A hydraulic cylinder that replaces the mechanical cable in a
               pedestal steering system. The cylinder is mounted at the
               quadrant and pushes/pulls the quadrant directly.
Vessel Size  : Sailboats 30–50 ft with existing cable-steered pedestals.
Rudder Auth. : Similar to single-cylinder ram; depends on quadrant radius
               and cylinder bore.

Operation    :
  - Functions identically to a single-cylinder ram in terms of hydraulic
    control
  - The key difference is MOUNTING: it attaches to the steering quadrant
    rather than having its own tiller arm
  - Many linear drives are self-contained units with integrated solenoid
    valves (e.g., Jabsco, Lewmar, Raymarine Type 1/2)

Autopilot Integration:
  - Self-contained units may have their own solenoid valves; the autopilot
    provides +12V to the appropriate port/starboard solenoid lines
  - Non-contained units require external solenoid valve blocks
  - IMPORTANT: Many linear drives have a clutch solenoid; this must be
    energized BEFORE driving the port/starboard solenoids

1.5  ROTARY HYDRAULIC ACTUATOR
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Description  : A hydraulic motor that drives the rudder stock through a
               gear reduction or direct vane mechanism. Rotates the rudder
               stock rather than pushing linearly.
Vessel Size  : Motor vessels and large sailboats (50+ ft) with rotary
               steering systems.
Rudder Auth. : High torque output, suitable for large rudder areas and
               high vessel displacements.

Operation    :
  - Hydraulic motor drives a reduction gear attached to the rudder stock
  - Solenoid valve controls flow direction → controls rotation direction
  - Speed is proportional to flow rate

Autopilot Control:
  - Same solenoid valve control scheme
  - May include a bypass valve for manual steering override
  - Rudder feedback is critical (absolute encoder preferred)

1.6  RUDDER THROW ANGLES — REFERENCE TABLE
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  System Type              | Min Throw | Max Throw | Typical       | Notes
  -------------------------|-----------|-----------|---------------|------------------
  Single-cylinder ram      | +/-30 deg | +/-45 deg | +/-35 deg     | Sailboats 25-45ft
  Dual-cylinder            | +/-35 deg | +/-45 deg | +/-40 deg     | Larger vessels
  Linear drive (cable rep) | +/-30 deg | +/-40 deg | +/-35 deg     | Pedestal steer
  Rotary actuator          | +/-35 deg | +/-45 deg | +/-40 deg     | Motor vessels
  Mechanical (reference)   | +/-35 deg | +/-45 deg | +/-40 deg     | Cable/chain

CRITICAL DESIGN LIMIT:
  The autopilot software MUST have configurable rudder limit angles.
  Hard mechanical stops prevent over-travel, but software limits should be
  set 2-3 degrees inside the mechanical stops to prevent hydraulic shock
  and mechanical stress when the rudder hits the stop under power.

  Recommended software limits: ±33 degrees (for ±35 degree mechanical)
                               ±38 degrees (for ±40 degree mechanical)

1.7  HYDRAULIC SYSTEM PRESSURES — REFERENCE TABLE
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  System Type              | Min PSI | Max PSI | Typical PSI | Notes
  -------------------------|---------|---------|-------------|---------------------
  Small sailboat (<30ft)   |   500   |  1500   |   800-1000  | Light loads
  Medium sailboat (30-45ft)|   800   |  2000   |  1200-1500  | Most common
  Large sailboat (>45ft)   |  1000   |  3000   |  1500-2000  | Heavy loads
  Motor vessel             |  1000   |  2500   |  1500-2000  | Continuous use
  Commercial               |  1500   |  3000   |  2000-2500  | Class requirements

The autopilot does NOT need to measure or regulate hydraulic pressure.
However, the solenoid valve must be rated for the MAXIMUM system pressure
with a safety margin of at least 1.5x. A system operating at 1500 PSI
requires valves rated for 2250+ PSI minimum.

Typical solenoid valve pressure ratings:
  - standard: 2000 PSI (adequate for most recreational)
  - high-pressure: 3000 PSI (commercial/large vessel)
  - hydraulic shock rating: typically 1.5× working pressure

2. SOLENOID VALVE CONTROL
================================================================================

2.1  THREE-POSITION FOUR-WAY SOLENOID VALVE (3P4W)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This is the standard hydraulic directional control valve for autopilots.

Configuration:
  - Three positions: PORT  |  CENTER (neutral)  |  STARBOARD
  - Four ports: P (pressure/pump), T (tank/reservoir), A (cylinder port side),
    B (cylinder starboard side)
  - Two solenoid coils: one for PORT, one for STARBOARD
  - Center position: all ports blocked (closed center) or connected to tank
    (open center)

Position Mapping:
  PORT solenoid energized:
    P → A (pressure to port side of cylinder)
    B → T (starboard side vents to tank)
    Result: Rudder moves to PORT

  STARBOARD solenoid energized:
    P → B (pressure to starboard side)
    A → T (port side vents to tank)
    Result: Rudder moves to STARBOARD

  BOTH DE-ENERGIZED (center):
    A, B, P, T all blocked → hydraulic lock
    Rudder holds current position (provided no external force exceeds
    the relief valve setting)

2.2  CENTER SPRING RETURN — FAIL-SAFE DESIGN
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

CRITICAL SAFETY FEATURE:

The solenoid valve has a mechanical spring that returns the spool to the
CENTER (neutral) position when BOTH solenoids are de-energized.

Fail-Safe Behavior:
  - Loss of power to ESP32 → both solenoids de-energize → valve returns
    to center → rudder holds position
  - Loss of power to MOSFET driver board → same result
  - Firmware crash → GPIO pins default to LOW → MOSFETs off → same result
  - ANY single failure that removes power from the solenoid coils results
    in the valve centering

This is the PRIMARY fail-safe mechanism for the hydraulic steering system.

Design Implication:
  - The MOSFET driver circuit MUST default to OUTPUT LOW (solenoid off)
    when the ESP32 is booting, resetting, or powered off
  - Use pull-down resistors on MOSFET gates (10KΩ to GND)
  - ESP32 GPIO configuration: set pins LOW before enabling output mode
  - Consider hardware watchdog on the MOSFET driver that cuts power if
    no valid PWM/enable signal is received

2.3  SOLENOID COIL CURRENT DRAW
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Typical Specifications for 12V DC Hydraulic Solenoid Coils:

  Parameter              | Value               | Notes
  -----------------------|---------------------|--------------------------------
  Coil resistance        | 6-12 ohms           | When cold; increases ~20% hot
  Inrush current         | 2.0-3.5 A           | First 50-100ms (coil cold)
  Holding current        | 1.0-2.0 A           | Steady state
  Power per coil         | 12-36 W             | Holding
  Total (both coils)     | 2.0-4.0 A           | Only ONE energized at a time
  Clutch solenoid        | 1.0-2.5 A           | Separate, when engaged
  TOTAL system max       | 6.5 A (worst case)  | Drive + clutch simultaneously

CRITICAL: Both PORT and STARBOARD solenoids must NEVER be energized
simultaneously. This would create a hydraulic dead-head condition
(pressurizing both sides of the cylinder simultaneously), which:
  - Wastes pump capacity
  - Creates excessive heat
  - May trigger the relief valve continuously
  - Could damage the cylinder seals

Software Interlock:
  In the ESP32 firmware, implement a HARDWARE-LEVEL interlock:
    if (port_solenoid_on) { starboard_solenoid = OFF; }
    if (starboard_solenoid_on) { port_solenoid = OFF; }

2.4  FLYBACK DIODE / FREEWHEELING DIODE CIRCUIT
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

THIS IS CRITICAL FOR ESP32 PROTECTION.

When a solenoid coil is de-energized, the collapsing magnetic field
generates a large voltage spike (back-EMF). This spike can easily reach
100-200V and WILL destroy the MOSFET and potentially the ESP32.

Solution: Flyback Diode (also called freewheeling diode or snubber diode)

Placement:
  - Cathode → solenoid positive (+12V) terminal
  - Anode   → solenoid ground (drain of MOSFET) terminal
  - The diode is in REVERSE bias during normal operation (no current flows)
  - When the MOSFET turns OFF, the back-EMF current circulates through
    the diode and decays exponentially

Diode Specifications:
  - Reverse voltage rating: minimum 2× supply voltage = 50V minimum
  - Forward current rating: equal to solenoid holding current = 3A minimum
  - Recovery time: fast recovery preferred (<500ns)

Recommended Diodes:
  1. 1N5408            — 3A, 1000V, general purpose (adequate, cheap)
  2. UF4007            — 1A, 1000V, ultra-fast recovery (better EMI)
  3. MUR820            — 8A, 200V, ultra-fast recovery (overkill but robust)
  4. SS34 (Schottky)   — 3A, 40V, very fast, low Vf (good if voltage < 24V)

SELECTION: For 12V systems with 3A solenoid coils, use UF4007.
           For 24V systems or high-current solenoids, use MUR820.

Placement Rule: One flyback diode PER solenoid coil. Three solenoids
(port, starboard, clutch) = three flyback diodes minimum.

Additional Protection:
  - Place a 100nF (0.1μF) ceramic capacitor across the solenoid terminals
    for additional EMI suppression
  - Place a 100Ω-220Ω resistor in series with the flyback diode to
    dampen the current decay (prevents ringing); this is OPTIONAL but
    recommended for EMI-sensitive environments

2.5  MOSFET vs RELAY — SWITCHING DEVICE COMPARISON
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

OPTION A: ELECTROMECHANICAL RELAY
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Advantages:
  - Simple to implement and understand
  - Complete galvanic isolation between control and load circuits
  - Can switch AC or DC
  - No heatsink required
  - Tolerant of voltage spikes (contact gap provides isolation)
  - Easy to find marine-rated relay modules

Disadvantages:
  - Switching time: 5-15ms (slow by control system standards)
  - Contact wear: limited life, typically 100K-1M cycles at rated load
  - Contact bounce: 1-5ms of bouncing during switching (causes EMI)
  - Coil power: relay coil itself draws 50-150mA
  - Not suitable for high-frequency switching
  - Contact arcing with inductive loads (shortens life)
  - Solder joints to relay terminals can fatigue under vibration

Suitable Relays for This Application:
  - Omron G8V-1: SPDT, 12V coil, 2A contacts, automotive grade
  - Panasonic TXS2: SPDT, 12V coil, 3A contacts, sealed
  - Bosch-type automotive relay: SPDT, 12V/40A (overkill but robust)
  - Songle SRD-05VDC-SL-C: common, cheap, 10A contacts (not marine-rated)

Recommendation for Relay:
  If using relays, use a dual SPDT relay module (two relays on one board).
  The flyback diode protects the relay contacts from the solenoid's
  inductive kickback. Add a snubber RC network (100Ω + 100nF) across
  the relay contacts for additional protection.

  Relay driving from ESP32:
    ESP32 GPIO → 1KΩ resistor → Transistor base (2N2222 or BC547)
    Transistor collector → Relay coil
    Relay coil → +12V
    Flyback diode across relay coil (1N4148 is adequate for relay coil)

  Total components per channel: 1 relay + 1 NPN transistor + 2 resistors
    + 1 diode (relay coil) + 1 diode (solenoid flyback)

OPTION B: MOSFET (RECOMMENDED)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Advantages:
  - Switching time: <1μs (effectively instant for autopilot purposes)
  - No moving parts = infinite mechanical life
  - No contact bounce = clean switching
  - Very low on-resistance → minimal voltage drop and heat generation
  - Silent operation (no clicking)
  - Can be PWM-controlled (if proportional valves are used)
  - Easy to parallel for higher current capacity

Disadvantages:
  - Vulnerable to overvoltage (back-EMF, ESD)
  - Requires gate drive circuit for optimal performance
  - Requires flyback diode for inductive loads (MANDATORY)
  - Not galvanically isolated (control and load share ground)
  - Sensitive to EMI on gate terminal

Recommended MOSFETs:

  1. IRLZ44N (Logic-Level, RECOMMENDED)
     - Vgs(th) = 1-2V (fully on at 5V gate drive)
     - Id(max) = 47A
     - Rds(on) = 22mΩ at Vgs=5V
     - Vds(max) = 55V
     - Package: TO-220
     - Cost: ~$1.50 each
     - ADVANTAGE: Can be driven directly from ESP32 3.3V GPIO (partial
       turn-on; fully on at 5V via logic level shifter or gate driver)

  2. IRF540N (Standard-Level)
     - Vgs(th) = 2-4V (needs 10V gate drive for full turn-on)
     - Id(max) = 33A
     - Rds(on) = 44mΩ at Vgs=10V
     - Vds(max) = 100V
     - Package: TO-220
     - Cost: ~$0.75 each
     - DISADVANTAGE: Cannot be fully driven from 3.3V or 5V alone;
       requires gate driver circuit or bootstrap charge pump

  3. IRL540N (Logic-Level variant of IRF540)
     - Vgs(th) = 1-2V (fully on at 5V)
     - Id(max) = 28A
     - Rds(on) = 77mΩ at Vgs=5V
     - DISADVANTAGE: Higher Rds(on) than IRLZ44N

RECOMMENDATION: Use IRLZ44N. It is logic-level, handles the current
easily, has very low Rds(on) at 5V gate drive, and is widely available.

Gate Drive Considerations:
  - ESP32 GPIO output: 3.3V
  - IRLZ44N at 3.3V Vgs: Rds(on) ≈ 50-60mΩ (acceptable but not optimal)
  - IRLZ44N at 5.0V Vgs: Rds(on) ≈ 22mΩ (much better)

  Options:
  a) Drive directly from 3.3V GPIO: Works but MOSFET runs slightly warmer
     (at 2A load: P = I²R = 4 × 0.06 = 0.24W — negligible, no heatsink)
  b) Level shifter to 5V: Optimal performance
  c) Dedicated gate driver IC (TC4420, MCP1416): Best but more complex

  For this application, option (a) is acceptable. The MOSFET dissipation
  is negligible even at 3.3V gate drive for 2-3A solenoid loads.

  HOWEVER, add a 10KΩ pull-down resistor from gate to source on each MOSFET
  to ensure the MOSFET turns OFF when the ESP32 is booting or the GPIO
  is in high-impedance state (input mode during boot).

RECOMMENDED APPROACH FOR THIS BUILD: IRLZ44N MOSFET, driven from ESP32
3.3V GPIO through a 100Ω series resistor (limits gate current spikes),
with 10KΩ pull-down resistor, and UF4007 flyback diode on each solenoid.

2.6  PWM FOR PROPORTIONAL VALVE CONTROL
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Standard solenoid valves are ON/OFF (bang-bang) devices. They are either
fully open or fully closed.

Proportional valves allow VARIABLE flow, enabling smoother rudder movement
and more precise control.

PWM on Proportional Valves:
  - PWM frequency: 100-500 Hz typical for hydraulic proportional valves
  - Duty cycle: 0-100% controls flow rate
  - The solenoid coil acts as an inductor; the PWM frequency must be high
    enough that the coil current is continuous (doesn't drop to zero between
    pulses) but low enough to avoid excessive switching losses

  - For most proportional hydraulic valves:
      Recommended PWM frequency: 200-300 Hz
      - At 200 Hz, the 12V solenoid coil (6Ω, 30mH) will have a time
        constant of τ = L/R = 30mH/6Ω = 5ms. Period = 1/200 = 5ms.
        Current ripple will be approximately ±37% of average — this is
        acceptable for proportional valve control.
      - At 300 Hz, period = 3.33ms, current ripple ≈ ±25% — smoother.

  - ESP32 has hardware LEDC PWM controllers capable of frequencies from
    ~10 Hz to 40 MHz, making this easily achievable in software.

  IMPORTANT — DITHER FREQUENCY:
    Many proportional valves are designed with a built-in dither signal
    (typically 50-200 Hz, 5-10% amplitude superimposed on the control signal)
    to overcome static friction in the valve spool. If the valve datasheet
    specifies dither requirements, implement them in firmware.

⚠ WARNING — PWM ON NON-PROPORTIONAL VALVES:
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
NEVER apply PWM to standard (non-proportional) solenoid valves unless the
valve manufacturer explicitly rates them for PWM operation.

Reasons:
  - Rapid switching causes the valve spool to chatter, wearing the spool
    and body surfaces
  - The coil's AC impedance at high PWM frequencies reduces effective
    current, potentially preventing the valve from fully opening
  - At certain frequencies, mechanical resonance can cause the spool to
    oscillate destructively
  - Eddy currents in the solenoid armature generate excessive heat
  - The valve may not fully seat when PWM is applied, causing internal
    leakage and loss of rudder holding ability

If using standard ON/OFF solenoid valves (which is the case for most
marine autopilot installations), drive them with a CLEAN DC signal —
no PWM, no modulation.

2.7  COMPLETE MOSFET DRIVER SCHEMATIC (ASCII)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

See Appendix A for the full schematic. Summary of the circuit per channel:

  ESP32 GPIO → [100Ω] → MOSFET Gate
  MOSFET Gate → [10KΩ] → GND (pull-down)
  MOSFET Drain → Solenoid (-) terminal
  Solenoid (+) terminal → +12V (fused)
  Flyback Diode: Cathode to Solenoid(+), Anode to Solenoid(-) / MOSFET Drain
  Optional: 100nF cap across solenoid terminals

  Three channels needed: PORT, STARBOARD, CLUTCH

  Gate Resistor Selection:
    - 100Ω series: Limits peak gate charge current from ESP32 GPIO
      (ESP32 max source current ≈ 40mA per pin)
      I_gate = (3.3V - Vgs_threshold) / 100Ω ≈ (3.3 - 2.0) / 100 ≈ 13mA
      This is well within ESP32 limits.
    - 10KΩ pull-down: Discharges gate capacitance when GPIO is floating.
      Time constant = 10KΩ × ~1nF (gate capacitance) = 10μs. This means
      the MOSFET turns off within ~30μs (3τ) of GPIO going floating.

3. CLUTCH / ENGAGEMENT MECHANISM
================================================================================

3.1  PURPOSE
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The clutch mechanism disengages the autopilot's hydraulic drive from the
rudder, allowing the helmsman to steer manually. When the clutch is
disengaged, the hydraulic cylinder is bypassed or mechanically decoupled,
and manual steering inputs (tiller or wheel) operate the rudder freely.

3.2  TYPES OF CLUTCH MECHANISMS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Type 1: SOLENOID-OPERATED HYDRAULIC BYPASS
  - A solenoid valve opens a bypass path in the hydraulic circuit
  - Fluid can flow freely between both sides of the cylinder
  - The rudder can be moved manually with minimal resistance
  - Common on linear hydraulic drives (Lewmar, Jabsco)
  - Clutch solenoid is typically a 2-way normally closed valve
  - When energized (12V applied), bypass opens → freewheel mode
  - When de-energized, bypass closes → autopilot engaged

  NOTE: Some systems are INVERTED — energized = engaged. Always check
  the specific drive unit's documentation.

Type 2: MECHANICAL CLUTCH (LEVER-OPERATED)
  - A mechanical lever physically disconnects the drive from the rudder
  - No electrical involvement
  - Autopilot cannot detect or control clutch state
  - REQUIRES a manual interlock switch (limit switch on the lever)

Type 3: MOTOR-DRIVEN CLUTCH
  - An electric motor moves a mechanical clutch
  - Takes longer to engage/disengage (1-3 seconds)
  - Requires position feedback to confirm engagement

3.3  AUTOPILOT CLUTCH CONTROL SEQUENCE
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ENGAGING AUTOPILOT:
  1. Helmsman presses AUTO button on the control head
  2. ESP32 verifies compass data is valid (heading known)
  3. ESP32 energizes clutch solenoid
  4. Wait 500ms-1000ms for clutch to fully engage
  5. Read rudder position (verify feedback sensor is responding)
  6. Capture current heading as target heading
  7. Enable PID control loop
  8. Display "AUTO" on OLED

DISENGAGING AUTOPILOT:
  1. Helmsman presses STANDBY button
  2. ESP32 disables PID control loop immediately
  3. ESP32 sets both PORT and STARBOARD solenoids OFF
  4. Wait 100ms for rudder to stabilize
  5. ESP32 de-energizes clutch solenoid
  6. Display "STANDBY" on OLED

EMERGENCY DISENGAGE (any button, power loss, or watchdog timeout):
  1. ALL solenoids OFF (port, starboard, clutch)
  2. Clutch disengages → helm free for manual steering
  3. Display "STANDBY" or flash warning

3.4  CLUTCH ENGAGEMENT DETECTION — FEEDBACK SWITCH
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

IMPORTANT: Do NOT assume the clutch has engaged just because the solenoid
was energized. The solenoid may be stuck, the hydraulic pressure may be
insufficient, or there may be a wiring fault.

Implementation Options:

Option A: CLUTCH SOLENOID CURRENT MONITORING
  - Measure current through clutch solenoid using ACS712 or current-sense
    resistor
  - Expected: 1.0-2.5A when engaged, 0A when disengaged
  - If commanded ON but current is 0A → clutch fault → alarm, no autopilot
  - Simple, no additional mechanical switch needed

Option B: PRESSURE SWITCH ON BYPASS LINE
  - A pressure switch on the bypass port detects when bypass is closed
    (clutch engaged)
  - Normally closed contact opens when bypass pressure rises
  - Connect to ESP32 GPIO with pull-up resistor

Option C: MECHANICAL LIMIT SWITCH
  - A microswitch or Hall effect sensor detects the physical position of
    the clutch mechanism
  - Most reliable positive indication
  - Requires mechanical installation on the drive unit

RECOMMENDATION: Use Option A (current monitoring) as the primary detection
method. It requires no additional mechanical installation and is implemented
in the existing circuit. Add Option B or C if the installation allows.

3.5  SAFETY INTERLOCKS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The firmware MUST enforce the following interlocks:

  1. NEVER drive port or starboard solenoids unless clutch is confirmed
     engaged (current > 0.5A measured on clutch circuit)
  2. If clutch engagement is lost during autopilot operation (current drops
     to zero), IMMEDIATELY deactivate port and starboard solenoids
  3. If rudder feedback sensor is out of range or not responding, do not
     engage autopilot
  4. Provide audible and visual alarm for any clutch fault

================================================================================
4. RUDDER FEEDBACK SENSOR DESIGN
================================================================================

4.1  PURPOSE
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The rudder feedback sensor provides the autopilot with the actual position
of the rudder. This is essential for:
  - Closed-loop PID control (the autopilot needs to know rudder position)
  - Rate limiting (calculating how fast the rudder is moving)
  - End-stop detection (software limits before mechanical stops)
  - Fault detection (rudder not moving when solenoid is commanded)
  - Display of rudder angle to the helmsman

4.2  OPTION A: POTENTIOMETER (10K LINEAR TAPER)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Description:
  A 10KΩ linear taper potentiometer mechanically linked to the rudder stock
  via a linkage arm or directly mounted on the rudder stock.

Specifications:
  - Resistance: 10KΩ (linear taper)
  - Travel: Typically 270°-300° of mechanical rotation for full resistance
    travel
  - Rudder throw: ±40° (80° total)
  - Resistance used: (80/300) × 10KΩ = 2.67KΩ of the total 10KΩ range
  - Mounting: Direct on rudder stock (preferred) or via bellcrank linkage

Voltage Divider Circuit:
                         +5V
                          |
                         [10K Potentiometer]
                          |
  ESP32 ADC ←────── wiper (middle terminal)
                          |
                         GND

  Voltage range:
    At amidships (center): V = 5V × (5K / 10K) = 2.5V
    At full port:          V = 2.5V - 1.33V = ~1.17V (depends on mounting)
    At full starboard:     V = 2.5V + 1.33V = ~3.83V

  ESP32 ADC specifications:
    - 12-bit ADC: 0-4095 counts over 0-3.3V (default attenuations)
    - For 0-5V range, use voltage divider or set ADC attenuation to 11dB
      (measures up to ~3.3V)
    - RECOMMENDATION: Use a dedicated ADC with 5V capability, OR use a
      voltage divider to scale 0-5V to 0-3.3V for the ESP32 ADC

  Voltage Divider for ESP32 ADC:
    Pot wiper → [6.8KΩ] → ESP32 ADC pin
                        → [3.3KΩ] → GND
    Ratio: 3.3 / (6.8 + 3.3) = 0.327 → 5V × 0.327 = 1.63V... NO.

  CORRECTED Voltage Divider:
    We want 5V → 3.3V. Ratio = 3.3/5.0 = 0.66
    R_top = 6.8KΩ, R_bottom = 13.3KΩ → ratio = 13.3/(6.8+13.3) = 0.662 ✓

    Or simpler: R_top = 10KΩ, R_bottom = 20KΩ → ratio = 20/(10+20) = 0.667 ✓
    5V → 3.33V (within ESP32 range at 11dB attenuation)

    RECOMMENDATION: 10KΩ + 20KΩ voltage divider before ESP32 ADC input.

Low-Pass Filter for Noise Rejection:
  Marine environments have significant electrical noise (engine alternator,
  VHF radio, radar, etc.). The potentiometer wiper signal must be filtered.

  RC Low-Pass Filter:
    ESP32 ADC pin → [1KΩ] → Filtered Signal → [100nF] → GND
    Cutoff frequency: f_c = 1 / (2π × R × C) = 1 / (2π × 1000 × 100e-9)
                    = 1,592 Hz

  This passes the rudder signal (which changes at <10 Hz) while rejecting
  high-frequency noise from alternators and other sources.

  For additional filtering, implement a software moving average or IIR
  filter in the ESP32 firmware:
    filtered_angle = 0.85 × filtered_angle + 0.15 × raw_angle
    (time constant ≈ 6 samples at 50Hz sample rate = 120ms)

Mounting Considerations:
  - MUST survive continuous exposure to salt spray, humidity, UV, and
    temperature cycling (-10°C to +60°C)
  - IP67 rating minimum (immersion to 1m for 30 minutes)
  - If the potentiometer itself is not IP67, mount it in a sealed enclosure
    with a waterproof shaft seal (e.g., IP68-rated cable gland or rotary
    seal)
  - Use a stainless steel (316) linkage rod with spherical rod-end bearings
    (Heim joints) to accommodate misalignment
  - The potentiometer shaft must NOT support any side load — use a separate
    support bearing on the rudder stock

Recommended Potentiometers:
  1. Bourns 6639 Series: Sealed, IP67, conductive plastic, 10K linear
     - Excellent for marine use if properly mounted
  2. ALPS Alpine RKJXK Series: Sealed, IP67, magnetic encoder option
  3. Piher PC-16 Series: Sealed, IP68, conductive plastic
  4. Honeywell 311 Series: Heavy-duty, sealed, IP68

CALIBRATION PROCEDURE:
  1. Physical Installation: Mount pot on rudder stock, set rudder to
     amidships (center). Adjust pot mounting so wiper reads approximately
     50% (center of resistance range).
  2. Electrical Calibration (via ESP32 menu):
     a. Set rudder to hard PORT stop (or known port angle, e.g., -35°)
     b. Press "CAL PORT" button → ESP32 stores ADC value as PORT_LIMIT
     c. Set rudder to hard STARBOARD stop (or known starboard angle, e.g., +35°)
     d. Press "CAL STBD" button → ESP32 stores ADC value as STBD_LIMIT
     e. Set rudder to amidships (center)
     f. Press "CAL CENTER" button → ESP32 stores ADC value as CENTER
  3. Software calculates linear mapping:
       angle = CENTER_ANGLE + (adc_value - CENTER_ADC) ×
               (ANGLE_RANGE / (STBD_ADC - PORT_ADC))
  4. Verify: slowly move rudder from hard port to hard starboard, verify
     displayed angle matches visual reference (protractor on rudder stock).
  5. Store calibration values in ESP32 NVS (non-volatile storage) so they
     persist across power cycles.

4.3  OPTION B: ABSOLUTE ENCODER — AS5048A (I2C INTERFACE)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Description:
  The AS5048A is a 14-bit magnetic rotary position sensor. It uses a magnet
  mounted on the rudder stock and a sensor PCB mounted adjacent to it.
  There is NO physical contact between the rotating and stationary parts.

Specifications:
  - Resolution: 14-bit = 16,384 counts per revolution
  - Angle resolution: 360° / 16,384 = 0.02197° ≈ 0.022° per count
  - Accuracy: ±0.05° (typical), ±0.1° (max over temperature)
  - Interface: I2C or PWM (I2C preferred for noise immunity)
  - Supply voltage: 3.3V or 5V (check specific variant)
  - Maximum air gap: ~3mm between magnet and sensor IC
  - Update rate: up to 14,545 Hz via I2C

Advantages over Potentiometer:
  - Non-contact: NO mechanical wear → infinite operational life
  - Immune to contamination (salt, dirt, moisture on the magnet don't matter)
  - No wiper noise or dead spots
  - Higher resolution and accuracy
  - True absolute position: reads correctly on power-up (no homing needed)
  - No voltage divider needed for ADC (digital I2C output)

Disadvantages:
  - Higher cost: ~$15-25 per unit (vs. ~$5-10 for marine potentiometer)
  - Requires I2C bus (2 wires + power + ground = 4 wires)
  - Magnet must be precisely positioned (axial alignment critical)
  - Requires PCB for the sensor IC and I2C pull-ups
  - Less tolerant of large axial misalignment between magnet and sensor

I2C Connection:
  AS5048A SDA  → ESP32 GPIO 21 (default I2C SDA) + 4.7KΩ pull-up to 3.3V
  AS5048A SCL  → ESP32 GPIO 22 (default I2C SCL) + 4.7KΩ pull-up to 3.3V
  AS5048A VDD  → 3.3V (or 5V depending on variant)
  AS5048A GND  → GND

I2C Address: 0x40 (default, can be changed via A1/A2 pins)

Software:
  - Use ESP32 I2C hardware peripheral (not bit-banged)
  - Read angle register (0xFE, 0xFF) — 14-bit value
  - Convert to degrees: angle_degrees = raw_value × (360.0 / 16384.0)
  - Apply offset calibration (magnet angular position vs. rudder amidships)

Calibration Procedure:
  1. Mount magnet on rudder stock (centered on rotation axis)
  2. Mount sensor PCB with correct air gap (1-2mm from magnet face)
  3. Power on, read raw angle at amidships → store as OFFSET
  4. Rudder angle = (raw_angle - OFFSET), wrapped to ±180°
  5. Move rudder to known port/starboard limits, verify angles match
  6. Store OFFSET in NVS

Marine Installation Notes:
  - Pot the entire AS5048A PCB in marine-grade epoxy (except the sensing
    face) for waterproofing
  - Use a neodymium magnet (N42 grade or higher) with temperature rating
    up to 80°C+
  - Mount the sensor housing in 316 stainless steel with a non-magnetic
    spacer (aluminum, brass, or Delrin)

RECOMMENDATION:
  For new builds or refits where reliability is paramount: Use AS5048A.
  For budget-conscious builds or retrofits: Use sealed potentiometer.
  For the Jetson-based autopilot with long-term reliability requirement:
  USE AS5048A. The marginal cost is justified by eliminating the primary
  failure mode (potentiometer wear).

================================================================================
5. RUDDER RATE LIMITER
================================================================================

5.1  PURPOSE
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The rudder rate limiter is a CRITICAL SAFETY mechanism that limits how fast
the autopilot can command the rudder to move. Even if the PID controller
outputs a full hard-over command, the software ramps the rudder angle at
a controlled rate.

5.2  WHY RATE LIMITING IS ESSENTIAL
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Mechanical Damage Prevention:
  - Sudden hard-over rudder movement (e.g., full port to full starboard
    in 1 second) generates enormous forces on the rudder stock, quadrant,
    tiller arm, and hydraulic cylinder
  - Repeated hard-over cycling causes fatigue cracking in rudder stock,
    quadrant bolts, and cylinder mount brackets
  - Hydraulic shock can damage seals in the cylinder and valve block

Crew Safety:
  - A rudder slam to hard-over on a sailing vessel at speed can cause
    violent broaching (vessel turning broadside to waves)
  - Crew below deck can be thrown against bulkheads
  - Gear and equipment can be thrown from shelves and berths
  - In extreme cases, broaching can lead to knockdown or capsize

Vessel Stability:
  - A sailboat's stability depends on the angle of heel relative to the
    wind and wave conditions
  - A sudden hard-over rudder movement can induce a roll that compounds
    with wave action
  - In following seas, this is particularly dangerous (broach / pitchpole)

Hydraulic System Protection:
  - Rapid cycling of the solenoid valves causes the hydraulic pump to
    cycle at maximum capacity
  - Continuous maximum pump operation generates heat → fluid overheating
  → reduced fluid viscosity → increased internal leakage → loss of
    rudder holding ability
  - Most hydraulic pumps have a duty cycle rating (e.g., 30% at max
    pressure); exceeding this causes premature pump failure

5.3  TYPICAL RATE LIMITS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Vessel Type          | Min Rate    | Max Rate    | Recommended  | Notes
  ----------------------|-------------|-------------|--------------|------------------
  Small sailboat <30ft  | 3 deg/sec   | 8 deg/sec   | 5 deg/sec    | Light rudder
  Medium sailboat 30-45 | 3 deg/sec   | 10 deg/sec  | 6 deg/sec    | Standard
  Large sailboat >45ft  | 2 deg/sec   | 8 deg/sec   | 5 deg/sec    | Heavy rudder
  Motor vessel          | 3 deg/sec   | 12 deg/sec  | 8 deg/sec    | Fast response
  Commercial            | 2 deg/sec   | 6 deg/sec   | 4 deg/sec    | Conservative

  Rate limits should be CONFIGURABLE in the autopilot firmware (via menu
  or serial command to Jetson).

5.4  IMPLEMENTATION
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Software Rate Limiter Algorithm (in ESP32 firmware):

  // Parameters
  float MAX_RUDDER_RATE = 6.0;  // degrees per second (configurable)
  float CONTROL_LOOP_PERIOD = 0.02;  // 50 Hz = 20ms per loop iteration

  float max_rudder_change_per_cycle = MAX_RUDDER_RATE * CONTROL_LOOP_PERIOD;
  // = 6.0 * 0.02 = 0.12 degrees per 20ms cycle

  // In PID control loop (called every 20ms):
  float pid_output;      // PID calculated rudder angle (degrees)
  float current_rudder;  // Current actual rudder position (from sensor)
  float commanded_angle; // Angle sent to solenoid valve (rate-limited)

  void apply_rate_limit(float target_angle) {
      float delta = target_angle - commanded_angle;

      if (abs(delta) > max_rudder_change_per_cycle) {
          // Rate limit: only change by the maximum allowed amount
          if (delta > 0) {
              commanded_angle += max_rudder_change_per_cycle;
          } else {
              commanded_angle -= max_rudder_change_per_cycle;
          }
      } else {
          // Within rate limit: go directly to target
          commanded_angle = target_angle;
      }

      // Apply hard limits (software end-stops)
      commanded_angle = constrain(commanded_angle, -MAX_RUDDER_ANGLE,
                                   MAX_RUDDER_ANGLE);

      // Drive solenoids based on commanded_angle vs current_rudder
      drive_solenoids(commanded_angle, current_rudder);
  }

5.5  ADDITIONAL RATE-BASED SAFETY FEATURES
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  1. RUDDER RATE MONITORING:
     - Calculate actual rudder rate: (current_angle - prev_angle) / dt
     - If actual rate EXCEEDS the rate limit (e.g., rudder moving faster
       than commanded), this indicates:
       a) External force on rudder (wave action, impact with debris)
       b) Hydraulic system fault (valve stuck open)
       c) Sensor fault (reading jumping)
     - Log the event; if persistent, trigger alarm

  2. DYNAMIC RATE ADJUSTMENT:
     - Reduce rate limit based on vessel speed (from GPS or paddle wheel)
     - At low speed (< 3 knots): allow faster rate (precise maneuvering)
     - At high speed (> 10 knots): reduce rate (prevent broaching)
     - Formula: effective_rate = base_rate × (1.0 - 0.5 × (speed / max_speed))
       where max_speed is configurable (e.g., 10 knots)

  3. HIGH-SEA-STATE RATE REDUCTION:
     - If the compass heading deviation exceeds a threshold (e.g., ±30°)
       for more than a few seconds, automatically reduce the rate limit
     - This prevents the autopilot from "fighting" large waves, which
       wastes hydraulic energy and can cause mechanical damage

================================================================================
6. OVER-CURRENT PROTECTION
================================================================================

6.1  PURPOSE
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Over-current protection monitors the current flowing through each solenoid
coil to detect faults:
  - Solenoid stuck ON (valve mechanically jammed)
  - Wiring short circuit (wire chafed, insulation failure)
  - Solenoid coil failure (open circuit = no current when commanded)
  - Connector corrosion (increased resistance, reduced current)

6.2  OPTION A: CURRENT SENSE RESISTOR (SHUNT)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Description:
  A low-value precision resistor placed in series with the solenoid's
  ground path (between MOSFET source and ground). The voltage across the
  resistor is proportional to the current through it: V = I × R.

Circuit:
  +12V → Solenoid (+) → Solenoid Coil → Solenoid (-) → MOSFET Drain
  MOSFET Source → [R_shunt] → GND
  Amplifier: V_shunt (across R_shunt) → differential amplifier → ESP32 ADC

Resistor Value:
  - For 3A maximum current, with desired 1V at full scale:
    R_shunt = V / I = 1.0 / 3.0 = 0.333Ω
  - Power dissipation: P = I²R = 9 × 0.333 = 3W
  - Use a 5W rated resistor (2× margin)
  - Standard value: 0.33Ω, 5W, 1% tolerance (wirewound or metal strip)

Amplifier:
  - The ESP32 ADC range is 0-3.3V
  - V_shunt at 3A = 3A × 0.33Ω = 0.99V (within range, but barely)
  - Use an op-amp with gain of 2-3 for better resolution:
    V_out = V_shunt × gain = 0.99 × 2.5 = 2.48V (good range in 0-3.3V)
  - Recommended op-amp: INA219 (I2C current/voltage sensor) or
    MAX471 (current sense amplifier with built-in gain)
  - Best choice: ACS712 module (see Option B) — simpler to implement

Advantages:
  - Very accurate
  - Low cost
  - No external power supply needed (passive component)

Disadvantages:
  - Requires precision amplifier circuit
  - Voltage drop across shunt (0.99V at 3A) reduces voltage available to
    solenoid (not significant for 12V system)
  - Needs PCB space for amplifier circuit

6.3  OPTION B: ACS712 HALL EFFECT CURRENT SENSOR (RECOMMENDED)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Description:
  The ACS712 is a Hall effect-based linear current sensor IC. It provides
  galvanic isolation between the current path and the output, and outputs
  an analog voltage proportional to the current.

Specifications:
  - ACS712-05: ±5A range, 185mV/A sensitivity
  - ACS712-20: ±20A range, 100mV/A sensitivity
  - ACS712-30: ±30A range, 66mV/A sensitivity

  RECOMMENDED: ACS712-05 (±5A) for solenoid monitoring. Best resolution
  for the 0-3A expected range.

Connection:
  ACS712 VCC → 5V
  ACS712 GND → GND
  ACS712 OUT → ESP32 ADC pin (through 1KΩ + 100nF low-pass filter)
  ACS712 IP+ → Solenoid (+) terminal (wire carrying current to measure)
  ACS712 IP- → Solenoid other terminal (load side)

Output Voltage:
  - At 0A: V_out = VCC/2 = 2.5V
  - At +3A: V_out = 2.5V + (3 × 0.185) = 3.055V
  - At -3A: V_out = 2.5V - (3 × 0.185) = 1.945V

  ESP32 ADC reading at 3.055V (with 11dB attenuation): ~3800 counts (out
  of 4095). Resolution: 5A/4095 = 1.22mA per count. Adequate.

Calibration:
  - Read ADC value with solenoid OFF (0A): store as ZERO_CURRENT_ADC
  - Calculate: current_A = (adc_reading - ZERO_CURRENT_ADC) × 5.0 / 4095 / 0.185

Implementation — Per Channel:
  - ONE ACS712 module per monitored channel (port, starboard, clutch)
  - Total: 3 ACS712 modules
  - Alternatively: monitor only the clutch solenoid for engagement
    detection and monitor the total supply current to the valve block
    for overcurrent detection (2 modules instead of 3)

6.4  FAULT DETECTION LOGIC
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Normal Operation Thresholds:
  Event                     | Expected Current   | Tolerance
  ---------------------------|--------------------|--------------------
  Solenoid OFF (idle)        | 0.0 A              | ±0.2A (noise)
  Solenoid ON (normal)       | 1.0-2.5 A          | Varies by unit
  Solenoid ON (hot)          | 0.8-2.0 A          | Decreases ~20%
  Clutch engaged             | 1.0-2.5 A          | Check datasheet
  Clutch disengaged         | 0.0 A              | ±0.2A

Fault Detection Rules:
  1. SOLENOID STUCK / SHORT CIRCUIT:
     If current > 4.0A for > 500ms when solenoid should be ON:
       → De-energize that solenoid immediately
       → Log fault: "PORT SOLENOID OVERCURRENT"
       → Sound alarm (buzzer + OLED warning)
       → Disengage clutch
       → Set autopilot to STANDBY
       → Send fault message to Jetson via serial

  2. SOLENOID FAILED OPEN:
     If current < 0.1A when solenoid is commanded ON for > 200ms:
       → Solenoid is not drawing current (open circuit)
       → Log fault: "PORT SOLENOID FAILED OPEN"
       → Sound alarm
       → Do NOT try to re-energize repeatedly (retries max 3, then lock out)

  3. CLUTCH FAULT:
     If clutch current < 0.1A when clutch is commanded ON:
       → Clutch not engaging
       → Do NOT allow port/starboard solenoids to activate
       → Log fault: "CLUTCH ENGAGEMENT FAILED"
       → Alarm

  4. WIRING FAULT (PARTIAL SHORT / HIGH RESISTANCE):
     If current is between 0.2A and 0.5A when solenoid should be ON:
       → Possible corrosion in connectors or partially damaged wire
       → Log warning: "HIGH RESISTANCE DETECTED — CHECK CONNECTIONS"
       → Continue operation but flag for maintenance

6.5  FUSING — HARDWARE OVERCURRENT PROTECTION
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Software protection is NOT sufficient alone. Hardware fuses must protect
against catastrophic failures.

Fuse Placement:
  1. BATTERY FUSE: 30A (at battery terminal, within 7" of battery post)
     Protects the entire autopilot power feed
  2. SOLENOID FUSE: 15A fast-blow (on the +12V wire feeding the solenoid
     valve block)
     Protects against short circuits in the solenoid wiring
  3. ESP32 FUSE: 5A fast-blow (on the +5V output of the buck converter)
     Protects the ESP32 and all low-voltage electronics
  4. CLUTCH FUSE: 5A fast-blow (on the clutch solenoid circuit)
     If clutch has a separate feed from the main solenoid circuit

Fuse Types for Marine Use:
  - ATO/ATC automotive blade fuses: cheap, readily available, adequate
    for 5-30A
  - MIDI or MEGA fuses: for higher current ratings, more robust terminals
  - ANL or MRBF fuses: for battery connection (30A+), ignition-protected

7.2  POWER DISTRIBUTION — DETAILED
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Source: Ship's 12V DC battery bank (lead-acid or LiFePO4)
  - Voltage range: 10.5V (discharged lead-acid) to 14.4V (charging)
  - For LiFePO4: 11.0V (discharged) to 14.6V (charging)
  - The buck converter must accept 10-16V input range

Wiring from Battery:
  1. Battery positive terminal → [30A ANL fuse, within 7" of terminal]
  2. Fuse output → 10 AWG tinned marine wire → main distribution point
     (at autopilot location)
  3. At distribution point:
     a. 18 AWG wire → 15A ATO fuse → solenoid valve block (+12V)
     b. 18 AWG wire → 5A ATO fuse → clutch solenoid (+12V)
     c. 16 AWG wire → buck converter input (+12V)
  4. Battery negative terminal → 10 AWG wire → common ground bus
     (at autopilot location)
  5. All grounds connect to common ground bus

Buck Converter:
  - Input: 10-16V DC
  - Output: 5V DC, 3A minimum (recommended 5A for headroom)
  - Recommended: Traco Power TSR 1-2450 (5V, 1A), or Recom R-785.0-0.5
    for ESP32 only, or a larger module (e.g., LM2596-based, 5V 3A) if
    powering multiple peripherals
  - Output → 5A ATO fuse → 5V rail (powers ESP32, OLED, encoder, sensors)

ESP32 Power:
  - ESP32 DevKit V1 has onboard 5V→3.3V LDO (AMS1117)
  - Feed 5V to the ESP32's VIN pin (not 3.3V pin)
  - ESP32 current draw: ~240mA (WiFi active) to ~50mA (deep sleep)
  - OLED current: ~20-40mA
  - AS5048A: ~10mA
  - ACS712: ~10mA each × 3 = 30mA
  - Total 5V load: ~350mA maximum (well within 3A supply)

7.3  MOSFET DRIVER BOARD WIRING

The MOSFET driver board contains three MOSFET channels (PORT, STBD, CLUTCH)
plus current monitoring.

7.5  COMPASS WIRING
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Assuming a BNO055 or similar I2C compass module:

  Compass Pin | Wire  | Destination
  -----------|-------|--------------------------------------------
  VIN        | 22AWG | +3.3V or +5V (check compass module spec)
  GND        | 22AWG | GND (common ground bus)
  SDA        | 22AWG | ESP32 GPIO 21 (shared I2C bus with encoder)
  SCL        | 22AWG | ESP32 GPIO 22 (shared I2C bus)

  NOTE: The compass and encoder share the same I2C bus. Ensure they have
  different I2C addresses (BNO055 default: 0x28 or 0x29; AS5048A default:
  0x40). These do not conflict.

7.6  OLED DISPLAY WIRING
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Assuming SSD1306 128x64 I2C OLED:

  OLED Pin | Wire  | Destination
  --------|-------|--------------------------------------------
  VCC      | 22AWG | +3.3V
  GND      | 22AWG | GND
  SCL      | 22AWG | ESP32 GPIO 22 (shared I2C bus)
  SDA      | 22AWG | ESP32 GPIO 21 (shared I2C bus)

  The OLED display shares the I2C bus with the compass and encoder.
  Three devices on one bus is well within I2C capabilities. Total bus
  capacitance should be kept under 400pF for reliable operation at 100kHz.

7.7  BUTTON WIRING
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Buttons are active-low (pressed = connect to GND, pulled HIGH by internal
ESP32 pull-up or external resistor).

  Button         | Wire  | ESP32 Pin  | Notes
  ---------------|-------|-----------|----------------------------------
  AUTO           | 22AWG | GPIO 32   | Engages autopilot
  STANDBY        | 22AWG | GPIO 33   | Disengages autopilot
  PORT -1 DEG    | 22AWG | GPIO 13   | Adjust heading target -1 deg
  STBD +1 DEG    | 22AWG | GPIO 14   | Adjust heading target +1 deg
  PORT -10 DEG   | 22AWG | GPIO 15   | Adjust heading target -10 deg
  STBD +10 DEG   | 22AWG | GPIO 16   | Adjust heading target +10 deg
  MODE           | 22AWG | GPIO 17   | Cycle through modes (menu/cal)

  All buttons: One side to GPIO, other side to GND.
  Enable INPUT_PULLUP in ESP32 firmware for each button GPIO.
  Software debounce: 50ms minimum (check button state, wait 50ms, check
  again; only register if both reads are LOW).

7.8  JETSON SERIAL CONNECTION
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  ESP32 UART2 TX (GPIO 17*) → Jetson RX (via level shifter)
  ESP32 UART2 RX (GPIO 16*) → Jetson TX (via level shifter)

  * Note: GPIO 16/17 may conflict with button wiring. If buttons are on
    these pins, move the UART to UART1 (GPIO 10/9) or use SoftwareSerial.
    RECOMMENDATION: Use UART1 (GPIO 10=TX, GPIO 9=RX) for Jetson comms,
    and keep GPIO 16/17 for buttons.

  Level Shifter: ESP32 is 3.3V logic; Jetson may be 3.3V or 1.8V.
    - If both are 3.3V: direct connection (with 100Ω series resistors)
    - If Jetson is 1.8V: use a bidirectional level shifter (TXB0108 or
      similar) or simple resistor divider (1KΩ + 2KΩ)

  Baud Rate: 115200 or 460800 (negotiated during startup)
  Protocol: Simple text-based commands (JSON or fixed format)
  Cable: Shielded twisted pair, max 10 meters

7.10  VOLTAGE DROP CALCULATIONS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Formula: V_drop = I × L × (2 × R_per_ft)
  Where I = current, L = one-way cable length, R_per_ft = resistance per foot,
  2 = accounts for both supply and return conductors.

  Example: Solenoid circuit, 25 ft cable run, 3A current
  R_18AWG = 6.385 Ω / 1000 ft = 0.006385 Ω/ft
  V_drop = 3.0 × 25 × (2 × 0.006385) = 3.0 × 25 × 0.01277 = 0.958V
  V_at_solenoid = 12.0 - 0.958 = 11.04V  → 7.98% drop

  This is ACCEPTABLE for solenoid operation (solenoids operate over 9-16V).
  If the drop exceeds 10%, use 16 AWG wire instead.

  Example: 5V sensor circuit, 10 ft cable run, 100mA current
  R_22AWG = 16.14 Ω / 1000 ft = 0.01614 Ω/ft
  V_drop = 0.1 × 10 × (2 × 0.01614) = 0.032V
  V_at_sensor = 5.0 - 0.032 = 4.968V  → 0.64% drop — EXCELLENT

  Voltage Drop Reference (18 AWG):
    Length (ft) | Current (A) | Voltage Drop (V) | Drop (%)
    -------------|-------------|------------------|--------
    5            | 3.0         | 0.19             | 1.6%
    10           | 3.0         | 0.38             | 3.2%
    15           | 3.0         | 0.57             | 4.8%
    20           | 3.0         | 0.77             | 6.4%
    25           | 3.0         | 0.96             | 8.0%
    30           | 3.0         | 1.15             | 9.6% ← LIMIT
    35           | 3.0         | 1.34             | 11.2% ← TOO HIGH

  RULE: Keep voltage drop under 3% for sensitive electronics (ESP32, sensors)
        Keep voltage drop under 10% for power devices (solenoids, pumps)

7.11  CONNECTOR TYPES — MARINE USE
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

All connectors in the autopilot wiring harness must be marine-grade:
  - Corrosion-resistant (tinned contacts)
  - Sealed against moisture (IP65+)
  - Vibration-resistant (locking mechanism)
  - Rated for the current and voltage of the circuit

Recommended Connector Types:

  For Power Circuits (solenoids, clutch):
    1. DEUTSCH DT Series: 2-12 pin, IP67, rated to 13A per pin
       - Industry standard in marine, automotive, and industrial
       - Tinned brass contacts, silicone seals
       - Part number example: DT06-2S (2-pin receptacle)
       - Requires DT series crimp tool (or solder contacts)
    2. AMPEHENOL AT Series: Similar to Deutsch DT, widely available
       - IP67, 13A per pin
    3. MARINCO / BEP Marine: Pre-made marine cable assemblies

  For Signal Circuits (sensors, buttons, serial):
    1. DEUTSCH DTM Series: Miniature version, IP67, 7.5A per pin
       - Better suited for signal wiring
    2. WATERPROOF M12 connectors: Industrial standard, IP67
       - Available in 4, 5, 8 pin configurations
       - Screw-locking, very robust
       - Good for sensor connections
    3. MIL-SPEC (MIL-DTL-38999): Overkill for recreational but standard
       for commercial/military

  For Internal PCB Connections:
    1. Molex KK series: Low-cost, reliable, 2.54mm pitch
       - NOT waterproof — use only inside sealed enclosure
    2. JST PH series: Compact, 2.0mm pitch
       - Good for low-current internal connections

RECOMMENDATION:
  - Use DEUTSCH DT series for all external connections (solenoid, clutch,
    power)
  - Use M12 connectors for sensor cables (compass, encoder)
  - Use Molex KK for internal PCB-to-PCB connections
  - Apply dielectric grease to all connector contacts during assembly

8. ENVIRONMENTAL PROTECTION
================================================================================

8.1  IP RATING REQUIREMENTS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The autopilot controller enclosure must meet a minimum of IP65:

  IP65: Dust-tight, protected against water jets from any direction
  IP66: Dust-tight, protected against powerful water jets
  IP67: Dust-tight, protected against immersion to 1m for 30 minutes
  IP68: Dust-tight, protected against continuous immersion

RECOMMENDATION: IP67 minimum for the autopilot controller enclosure.

Rationale:
  - The enclosure may be located in a cockpit locker, lazarette, or
    below-decks navigation station
  - These locations are subject to:
    - Occasional spray or drip (hatches, leaks)
    - High humidity (condensation)
    - Flooding (bilge water, hull breach)
  - IP67 ensures the electronics survive even if the vessel takes water
    into the enclosure area

Location-Specific Recommendations:
  - Cockpit installation: IP67 minimum (direct exposure to rain and spray)
  - Below-decks (dry): IP65 acceptable
  - Below-decks (potentially wet): IP67
  - Engine room: IP67 + additional thermal management

8.2  ENCLOSURE DESIGN
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Material Options:
  1. ABS plastic with gasket: Inexpensive, adequate for IP65
  2. Polycarbonate: UV resistant, good for cockpit mounting
  3. Fiberglass (GRP): Excellent for marine, strong, non-conductive
  4. Aluminum (6061-T6, anodized): Strong, conductive (EMI shielding)
  5. 316 Stainless steel: Maximum durability, heavy, expensive

RECOMMENDATION: Polycarbonate or fiberglass enclosure with silicone gasket.

Gasket Design:
  - Silicone rubber gasket, 2-3mm thick
  - Compressed 25-35% by enclosure lid bolts
  - Gasket groove in either the lid or the body (not both)
  - Use marine-grade silicone sealant on gasket during assembly

Cable Entries:
  - Cable glands (IP68 rated) for each cable entering the enclosure
  - Size each gland for the cable diameter; use silicone sealant around
    the cable inside the gland
  - Alternative: pre-made Deutsch connector bulkhead mounts
  - All cable entries must be at the BOTTOM of the enclosure (water
    runs down, not up)

Mounting:
  - Mount enclosure with 316 stainless steel bolts and nyloc nuts
  - Use rubber vibration isolators if mounted near engine or machinery
  - Ensure enclosure is accessible for maintenance and fuse replacement

8.3  PCB PROTECTION
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Conformal Coating:
  - Apply conformal coating to ALL PCBs in the autopilot system
  - Types (in order of preference for marine):
    1. Acrylic (AR): Easy to apply, easy to rework, good moisture barrier
       - MG Chemicals 419B or similar
       - Spray or brush application
       - Reapply annually in tropical/saltwater environments
    2. Silicone (SR): Excellent flexibility, good temperature range
       - Superior for vibration environments
       - Harder to rework
    3. Polyurethane (UR): Best chemical resistance
       - Excellent for fuel/engine room installations
       - Very difficult to rework

  Application Procedure:
    1. Assemble and test PCB completely
    2. Mask off connectors, switches, potentiometers, and any components
       that should not be coated (LEDs, displays, adjustment screws)
    3. Clean PCB with isopropyl alcohol to remove flux residues
    4. Apply conformal coating per manufacturer instructions
    5. Cure per spec (typically 24 hours at room temperature)
    6. Inspect for coverage (UV inspection light for acrylic coatings)

  PCB Material:
    - FR-4 with 1oz copper minimum (standard for most PCB fabricators)
    - Consider 2oz copper for high-current traces (solenoid MOSFET paths)
    - Minimum trace width for solenoid drive: 50 mil (1.27mm) for 3A
    - Minimum via size: 0.8mm (32 mil) for power traces
    - Solder mask: LPI (Liquid Photoimageable) green
    - Silkscreen: White, for component identification and installation marks

8.4  HARDWARE MATERIALS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Fasteners:
  - ALL external hardware: 316 stainless steel (A4 in metric notation)
  - Internal hardware (inside sealed enclosure): 304 stainless acceptable
  - NEVER use plain steel, zinc-plated steel, or brass where exposed to
    saltwater atmosphere
  - Nyloc nuts or thread-locking compound (Loctite 243) on all vibrating
    connections

Mounting Hardware:
  - Standoffs: 316 SS or nylon (for PCB mounting inside enclosure)
  - Washers: 316 SS, fender washers for cable strain relief
  - Cable ties: UV-stabilized nylon (natural, not black — black absorbs
    more UV and becomes brittle faster in marine environments)

Heat Sinks:
  - If MOSFET heatsink is needed (unlikely for this application):
    Use anodized aluminum with thermal compound
  - Anodizing provides corrosion resistance

8.5  WIRING MATERIALS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Wire:
  - MUST be tinned copper (not bare copper)
  - Tinning prevents corrosion wicking under insulation
  - Stranded wire (NOT solid) for vibration resistance
  - Minimum strand count: 19 strands for 22 AWG, 65+ strands for 18 AWG
  - Insulation: PVC (general purpose) or XLPE (high temperature, engine
    room)
  - Approved marine wire types:
    - UL 1426 (Boat Cable) — standard for marine
    - BC5W2 (tinned, multi-strand) — common designation
    - MIL-W-22759 — military specification, excellent but expensive

DO NOT USE:
  - Automotive primary wire (GPT, SGT) — thinner insulation, not rated
    for marine environment
  - Speaker wire — not rated for marine, poor insulation
  - Solid core wire — breaks under vibration
  - Untinned copper wire — corrodes rapidly in marine atmosphere

Terminals:
  - Tinned copper ring terminals for all connections to studs/terminals
  - Tinned copper spade terminals for quick-disconnect connections
  - Adhesive-lined heat shrink tubing on ALL terminal connections
    (e.g., 3M adhesive-lined heat shrink, or double-wall heat shrink)
  - Heat shrink provides:
    a) Mechanical strain relief (prevents wire fatigue at terminal)
    b) Environmental seal (adhesive lining bonds to wire insulation)
    c) Electrical insulation (covers any exposed conductor)

Terminal Crimping:
  - Use a proper ratcheting crimp tool (not pliers!)
  - Recommended: Ancor marine crimp tool or equivalent
  - Crimp tool must match the terminal size (red=22-16AWG, blue=16-14AWG,
    yellow=12-10AWG)
  - Pull-test every crimp: give a firm tug — the wire should NOT come out

8.6  CORROSION PREVENTION
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Dielectric Grease:
  - Apply to ALL connector contacts during assembly
  - Use marine-grade dielectric grease (not regular automotive grease)
  - Prevents galvanic corrosion between dissimilar metals
  - Repels moisture from contact surfaces
  - Does NOT interfere with electrical conductivity
  - Reapply annually during maintenance inspection

Cathodic Protection:
  - The autopilot enclosure should not significantly affect the vessel's
    cathodic protection system
  - If using an aluminum enclosure, ensure it is electrically isolated from
    the vessel's bonding system (unless intentionally bonded)
  - Do NOT create new ground paths through the autopilot wiring

8.7  MAINTENANCE SCHEDULE
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Interval    | Task
  -----------|---------------------------------------------------------------
  Monthly     | Visual inspection of all external wiring and connectors
  Quarterly   | Check connector tightness, clean any visible corrosion
  Semi-annual | Inspect enclosure gasket, reseal if necessary
  Annual      | Reapply dielectric grease to all connectors
  Annual      | Inspect and reapply conformal coating if thin/damaged
  Annual      | Test all fuses (replace any that have darkened)
  2 years     | Replace solenoid valve seals (preventative maintenance)
  5 years     | Replace potentiometer (if used) regardless of apparent condition
  As needed   | Replace any wire showing signs of chafing, corrosion, or
              | stiffening
