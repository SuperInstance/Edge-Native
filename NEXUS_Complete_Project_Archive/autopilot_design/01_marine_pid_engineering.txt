SECTION 1: BOAT STEERING DYNAMICS MODEL
================================================================================

1.1 OVERVIEW
------------
The marine autopilot control problem involves commanding rudder angle to
achieve and maintain a desired vessel heading. This is fundamentally a
course-keeping (regulator) problem with disturbance rejection requirements.

The plant (vessel + steering gear) is a SISO (Single-Input Single-Output)
system:
  - INPUT:  Commanded rudder angle delta_c  [degrees]
  - OUTPUT: Vessel heading psi             [degrees]
  - DISTURBANCES: Wind force, current, waves, propeller wash

The steering actuator (hydraulic cylinder driving a rudder stock) introduces
its own dynamics: deadband, rate limiting, and delay.

1.2 TRANSFER FUNCTION — 35FT SAILBOAT (TYPICAL)

The rudder-to-heading dynamics of a 35ft sailboat with hydraulic steering can
be modeled as a second-order system with a gain, two poles, and an effective
time delay:

         K_boat * omega_n^2
  G(s) = -------------------  * e^(-s*T_delay)
         s^2 + 2*zeta*omega_n*s + omega_n^2

Where:
  K_boat   = Open-loop heading rate gain [deg heading / deg rudder / sec]
  omega_n  = Natural frequency [rad/sec]
  zeta     = Damping ratio [dimensionless]
  T_delay  = Total loop delay [sec] (hydraulic + computation + sensor)

TYPICAL VALUES for a 35ft sloop, hydraulic steering, moderate displacement
(~10,000 lbs / 4,500 kg):

  Parameter          Value           Notes
  ----------         -----           -----
  K_boat             0.8 - 1.5      deg/s per deg rudder at 5 kts
  omega_n            0.05 - 0.12    rad/s (period ~ 50-125 sec)
  zeta               0.15 - 0.35    Underdamped (sailboats yaw naturally)
  T_delay            0.3 - 0.8      sec (hydraulic valve + fluid transit)

At higher speeds (8 kts), K_boat increases to 1.8 - 3.0 deg/s/deg due to
greater water flow over the rudder. The natural frequency omega_n also
increases proportionally with speed.

DERIVED QUANTITIES:

  Rise time (10-90%)  approx  = 1.8 / omega_n = 15 - 36 seconds
  Settling time (2%)  approx  = 4.0 / (zeta * omega_n) = 95 - 533 seconds
  Peak overshoot      approx  = e^(-pi*zeta/sqrt(1-zeta^2)) = 30% - 60%

These numbers explain why marine autopilot tuning feels sluggish compared to
industrial PID: the plant itself is slow and underdamped.

1.3 FIRST-ORDER APPROXIMATION

For control design purposes, the heading response can be simplified to a
first-order model with delay when the vessel is well-trimmed and seas are
moderate:

         K_boat
  G(s) = ------- * e^(-s*T_delay)
         T_s * s + 1

Where T_s is the dominant time constant (20 - 60 seconds for a 35ft boat).

This first-order model is useful for initial PID tuning and stability analysis.
The second-order model must be used for detailed performance prediction and
gain margin analysis.

1.4 SPEED DEPENDENCE OF RUDDER AUTHORITY
-----------------------------------------

Rudder lift force is proportional to V^2 (boat speed squared). This means:

  Effective_gain = K_base * (V / V_ref)^2

Where:
  V_ref  = reference speed (typically 5 kts)
  V      = actual speed through water
  K_base = measured gain at V_ref

PRACTICAL TABLE — Rudder Authority vs. Speed (35ft vessel):

  Boat Speed    Relative Gain     Notes
  ---------     -------------     -----
  2 kts         0.16              Almost no authority; AP will struggle
  3 kts         0.36              Minimal; large heading errors expected
  5 kts         1.00              Baseline; nominal tuning point
  7 kts         1.96              Good authority; can reduce gains slightly
  10 kts        4.00              Excellent; reduce gains to prevent oscillation

DESIGN IMPLICATION:
  The autopilot MUST apply gain scheduling based on water speed (STW from
  $VHW or log instrument). A controller tuned for 6 kts will oscillate
  dangerously at 10 kts and will be sluggish/inadequate at 3 kts.

  Gain scheduling factor:  f(V) = (V / V_tune)^2

  Apply to Kp:  Kp_effective = Kp_nominal * f(V)
  Apply to Ki:  Ki_effective = Ki_nominal * f(V)
  Apply to Kd:  Kd_effective = Kd_nominal * f(V)  (optional; some systems
              keep Kd constant for damping consistency)

1.5 WIND AND CURRENT DISTURBANCE MODEL
---------------------------------------

Wind and current act as nearly-constant disturbance forces on the vessel.
These are modeled as additive inputs to the heading dynamics:

  heading_dynamics:  psi_dot = f(delta, V) + d_wind + d_current

WIND DISTURBANCE:
  d_wind  [deg/s] = (K_wind * V_wind^2 * cos(alpha)) / (displacement * V_boat)

  Where:
    K_wind   = aerodynamic coupling coefficient (~0.005 - 0.02 for typical
                sailboat above-water profile)
    V_wind   = apparent wind speed [kts]
    alpha    = angle between apparent wind and vessel heading [deg]
    displacement = vessel displacement [kg]

  A 35ft sailboat in 25 kts of beam wind at 5 kts boat speed experiences
  approximately 0.5 - 2.0 deg/s of steady heading disturbance. This requires
  constant rudder deflection to counteract — this is the PRIMARY justification
  for the integral term in the PID controller.

CURRENT DISTURBANCE:
  Current does NOT cause heading error directly (the boat rotates with the
  water). However, current creates cross-track error (XTE) when following a
  GPS course. This is handled at the navigation layer, not the heading loop.

  The heading loop will command a heading offset to compensate for current:
    heading_command = course_over_ground + current_correction_angle

  current_correction_angle = arcsin( V_current * sin(current_angle) / V_boat )

WAVE DISTURBANCE:
  Waves are periodic disturbances modeled as:
    d_wave(t) = A_wave * sin(2*pi*f_wave*t + phi)

  Typical sea state frequencies: 0.08 - 0.2 Hz (period 5-12 seconds)
  Typical heading excitation:   2 - 8 degrees peak-to-peak in Sea State 4

  The PID controller must NOT try to follow wave-induced heading oscillations.
  The derivative term and a low-pass filter on the heading error are used to
  reject these high-frequency disturbances.

1.6 SECOND-ORDER VS FIRST-ORDER RESPONSE
-----------------------------------------

FIRST-ORDER RESPONSE (overdamped/heavy vessel, powerboat):
  - No overshoot
  - Slower convergence
  - Easier to control
  - Typical zeta > 0.7, omega_n = 0.03 - 0.06 rad/s
  - 40-60ft displacement powerboats often exhibit this behavior

SECOND-ORDER RESPONSE (underdamped, sailboat):
  - Overshoot present (30-60% without derivative control)
  - Faster initial response but oscillatory
  - Requires derivative gain for damping
  - Typical zeta = 0.1 - 0.4, omega_n = 0.06 - 0.15 rad/s
  - Most sailboats 25-50ft exhibit this behavior

DESIGN RULE:
  - If zeta > 0.5:  Use PI control (derivative not needed, may cause noise)
  - If zeta < 0.5:  Use PID control (derivative essential for stability)
  - If unknown:     Start with PID, Kd can always be set to zero

  A safe approach for an unknown vessel: begin with Kd = 0 and PI control,
  then enable Kd only if heading overshoot exceeds 10 degrees or oscillations
  persist for more than 3 cycles.

1.7 COMPLETE PLANT MODEL WITH ACTUATOR DYNAMICS

The full system includes actuator dynamics:

                              K_actuator
  Actuator:  G_act(s) = -----------------------
                        (T_act * s + 1)

  K_actuator = 1.0 (normalized: commanded angle = achieved angle, within limits)
  T_act      = 0.1 - 0.3 sec (hydraulic valve response time)

  Rudder rate limit:  |d(delta)/dt| <= delta_dot_max
    Typical values:   3 - 8 deg/sec for hydraulic systems
    This is set by pump flow rate and cylinder volume.

  Rudder travel limit: |delta| <= delta_max
    Typical values:    +/- 35 degrees (mechanical stops on rudder stock)

FULL OPEN-LOOP TRANSFER FUNCTION:

         K_boat * omega_n^2 * K_actuator
  G(s) = ---------------------------------- * e^(-s*T_delay)
         (T_act*s + 1)(s^2 + 2*zeta*omega_n*s + omega_n^2)

SECTION 2: PID LOOP DESIGN FOR HEADING HOLD
================================================================================

2.1 PID CONTROLLER FORMULATION
-------------------------------

The heading-hold PID controller operates on heading error:

  e(t) = psi_command - psi_actual    [degrees]

Heading error is normalized to the range [-180, +180] degrees:

  e_norm = ((e + 180) % 360) - 180

  Where % denotes the modulo (remainder) operation.
  Example: e = -200 deg  =>  e_norm = ((-200+180)%360)-180 = (-20%360)-180
           Python: e_norm = (e + 180) % 360 - 180

STANDARD POSITIONAL PID:

              1              de
  u(t) = Kp*e + Ki * INT e dt + Kd * --
                            dt

Where:
  u(t) = commanded rudder angle [degrees]
  e    = heading error [degrees], normalized [-180, +180]
  Kp   = Proportional gain [deg rudder / deg heading error]
  Ki   = Integral gain [deg rudder / (deg heading error * sec)]
  Kd   = Derivative gain [deg rudder / (deg heading rate)]

2.2 WHY THE INTEGRAL TERM IS ESSENTIAL
---------------------------------------

THE PROBLEM:
  Without the integral term, a constant disturbance (wind, current, asymmetric
  hull) produces a STEADY-STATE HEADING ERROR (offset).

  Steady-state error for PI controller:
    e_ss = d_disturbance / Kp

  With Kp = 2.0 and a wind disturbance requiring 5 deg rudder to counteract:
    e_ss = 5 / 2.0 = 2.5 degrees of permanent heading offset

  For a boat sailing 100 nautical miles, 2.5 degrees of heading error produces:
    XTE = 100 * sin(2.5 deg) = 4.36 nautical miles of cross-track error!

THE SOLUTION:
  The integral term accumulates error over time and produces a steady rudder
  command that exactly counteracts the disturbance:

    Ki * INT(e) dt = d_disturbance / 1  (at steady state)
    => steady-state error = 0

THE DANGER:
  If the boat makes a large course change (e.g., skipper turns 90 degrees) or
  if the compass glitches, the integral term can WIND UP to an enormous value
  during the transient. When the heading error finally reverses, the integral
  term drives the rudder hard over, potentially causing a broach or grounding.

  ANTI-WINDUP IS NOT OPTIONAL — IT IS A SAFETY REQUIREMENT.

2.3 WHY THE DERIVATIVE TERM IS ESSENTIAL
----------------------------------------

THE PROBLEM:
  An underdamped vessel (most sailboats) has natural yaw oscillations.
  Without derivative damping, the proportional term alone causes:
  - Overshoot of 30-60% on heading changes
  - Oscillation that decays very slowly (may persist for 5+ minutes)
  - "Hunting" behavior where the boat weaves back and forth

THE SOLUTION:
  The derivative term acts as a rate damper:
  - When heading error is increasing rapidly (boat turning away from target),
    the derivative term ADDS rudder in the corrective direction
  - When heading error is decreasing (boat turning toward target), the
    derivative term REDUCES rudder to prevent overshoot

  The derivative term is equivalent to adding viscous damping to the system.
  It increases the effective damping ratio zeta.

  Effective damping with derivative control:
    zeta_eff = zeta + Kd * omega_n / (2 * K_boat)

  For a 35ft sailboat with zeta = 0.2, omega_n = 0.08, K_boat = 1.0:
    With Kd = 10:  zeta_eff = 0.2 + 10*0.08/(2*1.0) = 0.6  (well damped!)

THE DANGER:
  The derivative term amplifies NOISE. Compass heading data from a fluxgate
  sensor typically has 1-3 degrees of noise at the sampling rate. Taking the
  derivative of noisy data produces large rudder commands that shake the
  steering.

  SOLUTION: Apply a first-order low-pass filter to the derivative path:
    de_filtered = alpha * de_new + (1 - alpha) * de_filtered_prev
    alpha = T_sample / (T_filter + T_sample)

    T_filter = 0.5 - 2.0 seconds is typical (cutoff freq = 0.08 - 0.3 Hz)

  Alternatively, compute the derivative on the HEADING signal (not the error)
  to avoid derivative kick on setpoint changes:
    u_d = -Kd * d(psi_actual)/dt

  This is called "derivative on measurement" and is STRONGLY RECOMMENDED for
  heading control, since setpoint changes (course changes commanded by the
  navigator) should not produce a derivative spike.

2.4 ANTI-WINDUP STRATEGIES
--------------------------

Multiple layers of protection are required:

STRATEGY 1: INTEGRAL CLAMP (Simplest, Required)

  Limit the integral accumulator to a maximum value:
    INT_e = clamp(INT_e, -INT_max, +INT_max)

  Where INT_max is set such that the integral term alone cannot drive the
  rudder beyond its limits:
    INT_max = delta_max / Ki

  Example: delta_max = 35 deg, Ki = 0.05:
    INT_max = 35 / 0.05 = 700 deg-sec

  This prevents the integral term from commanding more rudder than is
  physically possible, but it's not sufficient by itself.

STRATEGY 2: CONDITIONAL INTEGRATION (Recommended)

  Only accumulate integral when ALL of these conditions are met:
    1. |e| < e_integral_threshold   (e.g., 15 degrees)
    2. Rudder is NOT at its travel limit
    3. Autopilot has been engaged for > T_int_lockout seconds (e.g., 5 sec)

  During course changes or large transients, the integral is frozen. It only
  operates during steady-state course-keeping, which is exactly when wind and
  current compensation is needed.

STRATEGY 3: BACK-CALCULATION ANTI-WINDUP (Advanced, Optional)

  When the rudder command saturates, reduce the integral accumulator:
    If |u| > delta_max:
      INT_e -= (|u| - delta_max) / Ki * K_back

  Where K_back = 1.0 / T_reset (T_reset = anti-windup time constant, 2-5 sec)

STRATEGY 4: INTEGRAL RESET ON MODE CHANGE (Required)

  When the autopilot mode changes (heading hold -> course hold, or any new
  heading command), RESET the integral accumulator to zero:
    INT_e = 0

  This prevents accumulated offset from a previous condition from causing
  a transient when conditions change.

STRATEGY 5: INTEGRAL DECAY (Recommended)

  Apply a slow decay to the integral term to prevent indefinite accumulation:
    INT_e = INT_e * (1 - Ki_decay * T_sample)

  Where Ki_decay = 0.0001 - 0.001 (decay time constant = 1000 - 10000 sec)

  This ensures very old integral contributions eventually fade, adapting to
  slowly changing conditions (wind shifts, current changes).

RECOMMENDED IMPLEMENTATION:
  Use Strategy 1 + 2 + 4 together. Strategy 3 if precision requirements
  demand it. Strategy 5 for long passages (>6 hours).

2.5 HEADING ERROR NORMALIZATION
-------------------------------

CRITICAL: Heading wraps around at +/-180 degrees. Naive subtraction will
produce incorrect errors for headings near the wrap point.

WRONG:
  e = psi_command - psi_actual      // Fails when signs differ

CORRECT:
  e_raw = psi_command - psi_actual
  e_norm = ((e_raw + 180 + 360*1000) % 360) - 180
  // The +360*1000 handles negative e_raw correctly in integer math

VERIFICATION:
  psi_cmd = 355, psi_act = 005:
    e_raw = 350, e_norm = ((350+180)%360)-180 = (170)-180 = -10  CORRECT
  psi_cmd = 005, psi_act = 355:
    e_raw = -350, e_norm = ((-350+180)%360)-180 = (-170%360)-180 = 190-180 = +10  CORRECT
  psi_cmd = 180, psi_act = 179:
    e_raw = 1, e_norm = 1  CORRECT
  psi_cmd = 000, psi_act = 179:
    e_raw = -179, e_norm = -179  CORRECT
  psi_cmd = 000, psi_act = 181:
    e_raw = -181, e_norm = ((-181+180)%360)-180 = (-1%360)-180 = 359-180 = +179  CORRECT

ADDITIONAL CHECK: Also normalize the commanded heading to [0, 360):
  psi_command = ((psi_command % 360) + 360) % 360

2.6 SAMPLE RATE SELECTION
--------------------------

RECOMMENDED SAMPLE RATE: 10 Hz (100 ms period)

RATIONALE:

  | Rate  | Advantages                          | Disadvantages              |
  |-------|-------------------------------------|----------------------------|
  | 1 Hz  | Minimal CPU load                    | Excessive phase lag (>30 deg|
  |       |                                     | at vessel natural freq)     |
  |       |                                     | Poor derivative calculation |
  |       |                                     | Poor disturbance rejection  |
  |-------|-------------------------------------|----------------------------|
  | 5 Hz  | Adequate for large vessels (>50ft)  | Marginal phase margin for   |
  |       |                                     | smaller, faster-responding  |
  |       |                                     | vessels                    |
  |-------|-------------------------------------|----------------------------|
  | 10 Hz | Sufficient for all vessels 30-60ft  | Slight excess for 60ft     |
  |       | Good derivative accuracy            | displacement hulls         |
  |       | Low phase lag (<10 deg)             |                            |
  |       | Matches NMEA update rate            |                            |
  |-------|-------------------------------------|----------------------------|
  | 20 Hz | Better derivative accuracy          | Diminishing returns        |
  |       |                                     | NMEA data doesn't update    |
  |       |                                     | this fast anyway            |
  |-------|-------------------------------------|----------------------------|
  | 100Hz | Excellent theoretical performance   | Wasteful                    |
  |       |                                     | No new sensor data          |
  |       |                                     | Hydraulic system can't     |
  |       |                                     | respond this fast           |
  |       |                                     | May excite solenoid chatter |

PHASE MARGIN ANALYSIS:

  Additional phase lag from sampling = omega * T_sample / 2

  At the vessel's natural frequency (omega_n = 0.1 rad/s):
    1 Hz:   phase_lag = 0.1 * 0.5 / 2 = 2.9 deg    (acceptable)
    10 Hz:  phase_lag = 0.1 * 0.1 / 2 = 0.29 deg   (negligible)

  At higher frequencies where stability matters (omega_c = 0.3 rad/s,
  crossover frequency):
    1 Hz:   phase_lag = 0.3 * 0.5 / 2 = 8.6 deg    (significant!)
    10 Hz:  phase_lag = 0.3 * 0.1 / 2 = 1.5 deg    (negligible)

CONCLUSION: 10 Hz provides excellent performance for all vessels in the
30-60ft class. Going below 5 Hz is not recommended for safety reasons.
Going above 20 Hz provides no benefit.

IMPORTANT: The NMEA heading data ($HDM/$HDT) typically updates at 5-10 Hz
from modern compasses (Raymarine, Garmin, B&G). The PID loop should run at
10 Hz and use the MOST RECENT heading data, interpolating or holding the
last value if no new data has arrived.

2.7 DEADBAND IMPLEMENTATION
---------------------------

PURPOSE:
  - Prevent continuous solenoid cycling for tiny heading errors (noise, waves)
  - Reduce hydraulic pump wear and power consumption
  - Reduce annoying "hunting" when the boat is essentially on course

IMPLEMENTATION:

  deadband_half = 1.0 to 2.0 degrees (typical, user-adjustable)

  if |e_norm| < deadband_half:
      u_pid = 0.0    // No rudder command
  else:
      e_effective = e_norm - sign(e_norm) * deadband_half
      u_pid = PID(e_effective)

  Where e_effective is the error AFTER removing the deadband.

NOTE: The integral term should still accumulate (slowly) even within the
deadband, so that it can build up wind/current compensation. However, the
integral gain within the deadband should be reduced (e.g., Ki_deadband = Ki/4)
to prevent slow drift.

ALTERNATIVE (LINEAR DEADBAND — preferred for smoothness):
  Instead of a hard deadband, use a linear ramp:
    gain_factor = 0.0  if |e| < db_inner
    gain_factor = (|e| - db_inner) / (db_outer - db_inner)  if db_inner <= |e| < db_outer
    gain_factor = 1.0  if |e| >= db_outer

    u = gain_factor * PID(e)

  Typical values:  db_inner = 0.5 deg, db_outer = 2.0 deg

HYDRAULIC SOLenoid CONSIDERATIONS:
  Many autopilots drive solenoid valves (on/off), not proportional valves.
  Solenoids should NOT be cycled faster than every 200-500 ms to prevent
  overheating and mechanical wear.

  Minimum solenoid pulse width:  100 ms
  Minimum solenoid off time:    200 ms
  Maximum continuous on time:   10-15 sec (see Section 5.2)

2.8 COMPLETE PID ALGORITHM (PSEUDOCODE)

```
CONSTANTS:
  T_sample     = 0.1        // 10 Hz sample rate
  Kp, Ki, Kd                // Tuned gains (gain-scheduled)
  delta_max    = 35.0       // Maximum rudder angle [deg]
  db_inner     = 0.5        // Inner deadband [deg]
  db_outer     = 2.0        // Outer deadband [deg]
  INT_max      = delta_max / Ki
  e_int_thresh = 15.0       // Integral enable threshold [deg]
  T_filter     = 1.0        // Derivative filter time constant [sec]
  T_int_lockout = 5.0       // Seconds after engage before integral active

STATE VARIABLES (persistent across calls):
  int_accum    = 0.0        // Integral accumulator
  psi_prev     = 0.0        // Previous heading (for derivative)
  der_filtered = 0.0        // Filtered derivative
  t_engaged    = 0.0        // Time since autopilot engaged
  u_prev       = 0.0        // Previous output (for rate limiting)

FUNCTION pid_heading_hold(psi_command, psi_actual, dt):
  // 1. Normalize heading error
  e_raw = psi_command - psi_actual
  e_norm = ((e_raw + 180 + 360000) % 360) - 180

  // 2. Update engaged time
  t_engaged += dt

  // 3. Compute derivative (on measurement, not error — avoids setpoint kick)
  de_dt = -(psi_actual - psi_prev) / dt    // Negative because error = cmd - actual
  // Low-pass filter
  alpha_f = dt / (T_filter + dt)
  der_filtered = alpha_f * de_dt + (1 - alpha_f) * der_filtered
  psi_prev = psi_actual

  // 4. Compute proportional and derivative terms
  u_p = Kp * e_norm
  u_d = Kd * der_filtered

  // 5. Compute integral term with conditional integration
  if |e_norm| < e_int_thresh AND t_engaged > T_int_lockout:
      int_accum += e_norm * dt
  int_accum = clamp(int_accum, -INT_max, +INT_max)
  u_i = Ki * int_accum

  // 6. Sum PID terms
  u_raw = u_p + u_i + u_d

  // 7. Apply linear deadband
  gain_factor = deadband_factor(e_norm, db_inner, db_outer)
  u_pid = gain_factor * u_raw

  // 8. Clamp to rudder limits
  u_out = clamp(u_pid, -delta_max, +delta_max)

  // 9. Rate limit rudder command (protect hydraulics)
  delta_dot_max = 8.0  // deg/sec
  u_out = clamp(u_out, u_prev - delta_dot_max*dt, u_prev + delta_dot_max*dt)
  u_prev = u_out

  return u_out
```

SECTION 3: ADVANCED STEERING MODES
================================================================================

3.1 HEADING HOLD MODE
---------------------
The simplest autopilot mode. The skipper sets a desired magnetic heading,
and the autopilot maintains it.

  INPUT:  Target heading from keypad, rotary encoder, or NMEA sentence
  SENSOR: Compass heading ($HDM for magnetic, $HDT for true)
  CONTROL: Single PID loop as described in Section 2
  NMEA IN:  $APHDG,<heading>,<off-course-err>*hh  (Raymarine)
            $HDM,<heading>,<M>*hh               (compass)
  NMEA OUT: $APB,... (if connected to chart plotter)

  Typical heading hold accuracy: +/- 2-5 degrees in calm conditions
  Acceptable accuracy:           +/- 5 degrees in Sea State 3
  Poor but expected:             +/- 10-15 degrees in Sea State 5+

3.2 COURSE HOLD / TRACK CONTROL MODE
-------------------------------------
Follow a great-circle or rhumb-line course between two waypoints. This mode
adds an outer navigation loop around the heading hold loop.

ARCHITECTURE:
                                              +-----------+
  GPS Course --->| Course  |  Heading  -->    | Heading    |--> Rudder
  (COG)          | Error   |  Command   -->   | Hold PID   |
  Waypoint  --->| Calc    |                   +-----------+
  (Bearing)      +-----------+
                       ^
                       |
                  Compass Heading

COURSE ERROR COMPUTATION:

  course_error = bearing_to_waypoint - COG

  Normalize to [-180, +180] degrees.

  heading_command = bearing_to_waypoint + course_correction

  Where course_correction is computed by a SECOND PI controller operating
  on cross-track error (see Section 3.3).

KEY DIFFERENCE FROM HEADING HOLD:
  - The commanded heading is continuously updated based on GPS position
  - Cross-track error is minimized, not just heading error
  - The boat may sail a different heading than the waypoint bearing to
    compensate for current and leeway

3.3 CROSS-TRACK ERROR (XTE) MINIMIZATION
-----------------------------------------

XTE is the perpendicular distance from the vessel's current position to the
desired track line between two waypoints.

  XTE [nm] = distance from vessel to nearest point on track line

  Sign convention:
    XTE > 0: Vessel is to STARBOARD of track
    XTE < 0: Vessel is to PORT of track

XTE CORRECTION ALGORITHM:

  The XTE controller modifies the heading command to steer back toward the
  track line. This is a SECOND LOOP (outer loop) with much slower dynamics:

    heading_correction [deg] = Kp_xte * XTE + Ki_xte * INT(XTE) dt

  Typical gain values:
    Kp_xte = 5.0 - 20.0  deg heading per nm of XTE
    Ki_xte = 0.01 - 0.05 deg heading per (nm * sec)

  RATIONALE: At 1 nm XTE, the heading correction should be approximately:
    Kp_xte = 10 deg/nm => 10 degree heading correction

    For a boat making 6 kts, this produces a cross-track correction rate of:
      V_cross = 6 * sin(10 deg) = 1.04 kts = 1.04 nm/hr

    Time to correct 1 nm XTE: ~58 minutes (this is correct — you do NOT want
    to turn sharply to correct XTE; gentle correction prevents over-correction)

  XTE CORRECTION LIMIT: Clamp heading_correction to +/- 30 degrees to prevent
  the autopilot from commanding an absurd heading to correct large XTE.

  XTE ALARM: Trigger alarm if |XTE| > XTE_alarm:
    Coastal:   XTE_alarm = 0.1 nm (200 yards)
    Offshore:  XTE_alarm = 0.5 nm
    Ocean:     XTE_alarm = 1.0 nm

3.4 $APB SENTENCE (AUTOPILLOT SENTENCE B) — DETAILED SPECIFICATION

This is the PRIMARY NMEA sentence used to communicate autopilot commands from
a chart plotter or navigation computer to the autopilot:

  $--APB,A,A,x.x,a,N,A,A,x.x,a,c--c,x.x,a,x.x,a,a*hh<CR><LF>

  FIELD  | NAME                  | DESCRIPTION
  -------|-----------------------|------------------------------------------
    1    | Status (Bearing)      | V = warning/data invalid
         |                       | A = data valid (bearing OK)
    2    | Status (Heading)      | V = warning/data invalid
         |                       | A = data valid (heading OK)
    3    | Bearing origin->WP    | Bearing from vessel to dest WP [degrees]
         |                       | (true)
    4    | Bearing reference     | T = true, M = magnetic
    5    | Bearing dest->WP      | Not used, always N
    6    | Bearing dest->origin  | Not used, always A
    7    | Bearing to dest       | Bearing to destination waypoint [degrees]
    8    | Bearing reference     | T = true, M = magnetic
    9    | Dest waypoint ID      | Waypoint identifier (e.g., "WPT003")
    10   | Bearing origin->WP    | Bearing from vessel to dest WP [degrees]
         |                       | (magnetic, if applicable)
    11   | Bearing reference     | M = magnetic (or blank)
    12   | Heading to steer      | CALCULATED HEADING TO STEER [degrees]
         |                       | (magnetic) — THIS IS THE KEY FIELD
    13   | Heading reference     | M = magnetic
    14   | Checksum              | *hh (hex checksum)

  EXAMPLE (OpenCPN output):
    $GPAPB,A,A,045.2,T,M,0.20,N,,WPT005,A,044.0,M,045.0,M*3D

    Interpretation:
    - Bearing to waypoint: 045.2 deg True
    - Heading to steer: 045.0 deg Magnetic (corrected for current/set)
    - Dest waypoint: WPT005

  NOTE: Field 12 (Heading to Steer) is the field the autopilot uses as its
  heading command in course/tracking mode. This value already includes XTE
  correction calculated by the navigation software.

3.5 WIND MODE (APPARENT WIND ANGLE HOLD)
-----------------------------------------

Wind mode holds a constant apparent wind angle (AWA) rather than a heading.
This is used primarily by sailboats to maintain optimal sail trim.

  INPUT:  Target AWA from wind instrument
  SENSOR: Current AWA from wind sensor ($MWV sentence)
  CONTROL: PID loop on AWA error (not heading error)

  e_awa = AWA_target - AWA_actual

  Wind mode is fundamentally different from heading hold because:
    1. Wind gusts cause TRUE heading changes that are DESIRED (the boat
       should head up in a gust to maintain constant AWA)
    2. The heading will wander as the wind shifts — this is correct behavior
    3. There is NO integral term for heading error (we don't want to return
       to a fixed heading)
    4. There IS an integral term for AWA error (to maintain consistent sail trim)

  WIND MODE PID:
    u(t) = Kp_awa * e_awa + Ki_awa * INT(e_awa dt) + Kd_awa * d(e_awa)/dt

  Typical wind mode gains (35ft sailboat):
    Kp_awa = 0.5 - 2.0  deg rudder / deg AWA error
    Ki_awa = 0.01 - 0.05
    Kd_awa = 2.0 - 8.0  (critical for gust damping)

  WIND SENSOR REQUIREMENTS:
    - Update rate: >= 5 Hz (gusts are fast)
    - Accuracy: +/- 3 degrees AWA
    - Must be calibrated for upwash and apparent wind calculation
    - Raw wind angle must be corrected for heel angle

  NMEA SENTENCES FOR WIND MODE:
    $MWV,<wind_angle>,<R|T>,<wind_speed>,<K|M|N>,A*hh
    $VWR,<wind_angle_L>,<R>,<wind_speed_L>,<N>,...
    $VWT,<wind_angle_T>,<T>,<wind_speed_T>,<N>,...

3.6 RATE OF TURN LIMITING
--------------------------

PURPOSE:
  Prevent the autopilot from commanding a turn rate that could:
  - Cause crew to lose balance or be thrown (safety)
  - Cause excessive heel on a sailboat (knockdown risk)
  - Cause passenger discomfort on a powerboat
  - Exceed the vessel's rudder authority at current speed

IMPLEMENTATION:

  Rate limit the heading command, not the rudder command:

    d(psi_cmd)/dt_max = ROT_max [deg/sec]

    Typical values:
      Powerboat (passenger comfort):  3.0 deg/sec
      Sailboat (moderate):            5.0 deg/sec
      Sailboat (aggressive):          8.0 deg/sec
      Commercial vessel:              2.0 - 3.0 deg/sec

  Also rate-limit the actual rudder command:
    d(delta)/dt_max = 5.0 - 8.0 deg/sec (see Section 1.7)

  IMPLEMENTATION:
    psi_cmd_limited = psi_cmd_prev + clamp(psi_cmd - psi_cmd_prev,
                                            -ROT_max*dt, +ROT_max*dt)

    When the autopilot receives a new heading command (e.g., 90-degree turn),
    it RAMPs to that heading at the maximum allowed rate:
      Time to turn 90 deg at 5 deg/sec = 18 seconds
      Time to turn 90 deg at 3 deg/sec = 30 seconds

  |-------------|--------------|-------------------|--------------|
  | Heading Hold| Heading [deg]| Compass ($HDM)    | $APHDG or UI |
  | Course Hold | Track (WP)   | GPS + Compass     | $APB         |

SECTION 4: NMEA 0183 AUTOPILOT SENTENCES

4.1 NMEA 0183 BASICS
---------------------

  ELECTRICAL SPECIFICATION:
    Standard:  EIA-422 (RS-422) differential signaling
    Voltage:   +5V differential (TX+ to TX-)
    Baud Rate: 4,800 bps (default, most common)
               38,400 bps (high-speed, some modern instruments)
    Data Bits: 8
    Parity:    None
    Stop Bits: 1
    Connector: Typically Raymarine SeaTalk NG, Garmin NMEA 2000 gateway,
               or bare RS-422 (DB-9 or terminal block)

  WIRING (RS-422):
    TX+ (A) : Transmit positive
    TX- (B) : Transmit negative (complement of A)
    RX+ (A) : Receive positive
    RX- (B) : Receive negative
    GND     : Signal ground (MUST be connected between devices)

  NOTE: Many consumer chart plotters use TTL-level NMEA (0-5V single-ended)
  rather than true RS-422. Check specifications. TTL NMEA can only drive
  1-2 listeners. RS-422 can drive up to 10 listeners.

  TALKER IDS (relevant to autopilot systems):
    GP  = GPS receiver
    EC  = Echo sounder (depth)
    HD  = Heading/direction sensor (compass)
    HN  = Heading north-seeking (gyrocompass)
    II  = Integrated instrumentation (B&G, etc.)
    SD  = Sounder, depth (Simrad)
    VD  = Speed/distance (speed log)
    WR  = Weather station (wind)
    AG  = Autopilot general (Raymarine, Garmin)

  LISTENER CAPABILITY:
    An NMEA 0183 device can have:
    - 1 talker port (transmits on one pair)
    - Up to 4 listener ports (receives on separate pairs)
    - A "combined" port can be switched between talk and listen

4.2 $HDM — HEADING MAGNETIC
----------------------------

  $--HDM,x.x,M*hh<CR><LF>

  FIELD  | NAME             | DESCRIPTION
  -------|------------------|------------------------------------------
    1    | Heading          | Vessel heading in degrees (0-359.9)
    2    | Reference        | M = magnetic (always M for HDM)
    3    | Checksum         | *hh

  EXAMPLE:  $HDHDM,045.5,M*2A

  UPDATE RATE: 10 Hz (modern compasses), 5 Hz (older)
  ACCURACY:    +/- 1 deg (gyro-stabilized), +/- 3 deg (fluxgate only)
  USE:         Primary heading source for autopilot heading hold

4.3 $HDT — HEADING TRUE
------------------------

  $--HDT,x.x,T*hh<CR><LF>

  FIELD  | NAME             | DESCRIPTION
  -------|------------------|------------------------------------------
    1    | Heading          | Vessel heading in degrees true (0-359.9)
    2    | Reference        | T = true (always T for HDT)
    3    | Checksum         | *hh

  EXAMPLE:  $HDHDT,047.2,T*27

  USE: Heading true. The autopilot must apply magnetic variation to convert
  between true and magnetic as needed.

  Variation correction:
    heading_magnetic = heading_true - variation
    (variation is positive for west, negative for east — by convention)

4.4 $RMB — RECOMMENDED MINIMUM NAVIGATION TO WAYPOINT
------------------------------------------------------

  $--RMB,A,x.x,a,c--c,c--c,llll.ll,a,yyyyy.yy,a,x.x,x.x,x.x,A,a*hh

  FIELD  | NAME                    | DESCRIPTION
  -------|-------------------------|------------------------------------------
    1    | Data status             | A = Active, V = Void (invalid)
    2    | XTE                     | Cross-track error [nm]
    3    | Direction to steer      | L = steer Left, R = steer Right
    4    | Origin waypoint ID      | From waypoint identifier
    5    | Destination waypoint ID | To waypoint identifier
    6    | Destination latitude    | DDmm.mm format
    7    | N/S                     | N or S
    8    | Destination longitude   | DDDmm.mm format
    9    | E/W                     | E or W
   10    | Range to dest           | Distance to destination [nm]
   11    | Bearing to dest         | True bearing to dest [deg]
   12    | Dest closing velocity   | Speed toward dest [kts]
   13    | Arrival status          | A = Arrival circle entered
         |                         | V = Not in arrival circle
   14    | Mode indicator          | A = Autonomous, D = Differential,
         |                         | E = Estimated, N = Not valid
   15    | Checksum                | *hh

  EXAMPLE:
    $GPRMB,A,0.23,L,WPT001,WPT002,3723.45,N,12207.60,W,12.4,045.2,5.3,V,A*3B

  USE: The $RMB sentence provides XTE and bearing to waypoint information.
  Some older autopilots use $RMB directly. Modern systems prefer $APB.

  XTE INTERPRETATION:
    Field 2 (XTE) is always positive.
    Field 3 (L/R) indicates which side of the track the vessel is on.
    L = vessel is LEFT (port) of track -> steer RIGHT to correct
    R = vessel is RIGHT (starboard) of track -> steer LEFT to correct

4.5 $VHW — WATER SPEED AND HEADING
-----------------------------------

  $--VHW,x.x,T,x.x,M,x.x,N,x.x,K*hh<CR><LF>

  FIELD  | NAME          | DESCRIPTION
  -------|---------------|------------------------------------------
    1    | Water speed   | Speed through water [kts]
    2    | Heading true  | (often blank)
    3    | Reference     | T = true (often blank)
    4    | Heading mag   | (often blank)
    5    | Reference     | M = magnetic (often blank)
    6    | Water speed   | Speed through water [kts] (repeat of field 1)
    7    | Speed units   | N = knots
    8    | Water speed   | Speed through water [km/h]
    9    | Speed units   | K = km/h
   10    | Checksum      | *hh

  EXAMPLE:  $VDVHW,,T,,M,5.2,N,9.6,K*5D

  USE: Provides speed through water (STW) for gain scheduling.
  CRITICAL: The autopilot MUST have water speed, not GPS speed (SOG), because:
    - GPS speed includes current effect (wrong for rudder authority)
    - Water speed determines rudder lift force (correct)
    - A boat motoring at 5 kts SOG into a 2 kt current has only 3 kts
      water speed and correspondingly less rudder authority

4.6 HOW OpenCPN GENERATES AUTOPILOT SENTENCES
----------------------------------------------

OpenCPN (Open Chart Plotter Navigator) is a widely-used open-source navigation
software that can output autopilot commands. Configuration:

  SETTINGS -> Connections -> NMEA -> Output:

  1. Enable "Output $APB sentences" ( checkbox)
  2. Enable "Output $RMB sentences" (checkbox, for older APs)
  3. Select the output port (serial or TCP)
  4. Set baud rate to 4800 (or match AP input)

  OpenCPN generates $APB when:
    - A route is active (navigating between waypoints)
    - The autopilot "follow" mode is enabled

  OpenCPN heading-to-steer calculation:
    1. Compute bearing to active waypoint (great circle or rhumb line)
    2. Compute current COG from GPS
    3. Calculate XTE from cross-product of track vector and position vector
    4. Apply XTE correction:  heading_correction = arcsin(XTE / distance_to_WP)
       (simplified; actual algorithm uses proportional control)
    5. Apply magnetic variation to convert true bearing to magnetic
    6. Output in $APB field 12

  OpenCPN does NOT generate $HDM or $VHW — it receives these from instruments.
  OpenCPN DOES generate $GPBOD (bearing origin to destination) and $GPBWC
  (bearing and distance to waypoint) which some autopilots accept.

4.7 HOW TIMEZERO OUTPUTS AUTOPILOT COMMANDS
-------------------------------------------

Nobeltec TimeZero (now MaxSea TimeZero) outputs autopilot commands via:

  OUTPUT FORMAT: NMEA 0183 (4800 baud) or NMEA 2000 (depending on version)
  PRIMARY SENTENCE: $APB (Autopilot Sentence B)
  SECONDARY: $RMB (for legacy autopilot compatibility)

  TimeZero configuration:
    1. Tools -> Options -> Connections -> NMEA Out
    2. Enable "APB" and/or "RMB" output
    3. Configure serial port (COM port on Windows, /dev/tty on Linux)
    4. Set baud rate: 4800 (standard NMEA)

  TimeZero computes heading-to-steer using:
    - Course over ground (COG) from GPS
    - Bearing to waypoint
    - Cross-track error with proportional correction
    - Magnetic variation from WMM model (built-in, updated annually)
    - User-configurable XTE gain and alarm limits

  TimeZero also outputs:
    - $GPXTE: Standalone XTE sentence (for APs that use it)
    - $GPBWC: Bearing and distance to waypoint
    - $GPBOD: Bearing origin to destination

SECTION 5: SAFETY-CRITICAL DESIGN
================================================================================

5.1 WATCHDOG TIMER REQUIREMENTS
--------------------------------

PURPOSE: Detect if the PID loop or processor has frozen. A frozen autopilot
with rudder hard-over is an immediate life-safety hazard.

REQUIREMENTS:

  1. HARDWARE WATCHDOG (Required):
     - Independent hardware timer (NOT software-only)
     - Must NOT be clearable by the main processor in a stuck state
     - Timeout: 200 - 500 ms (2-5 PID cycles at 10 Hz)
     - On timeout: DE-ENERGIZE all solenoid outputs (fail to center/neutral)
     - Must trigger a visible and audible alarm

  2. SOFTWARE WATCHDOG (Additional layer):
     - Main loop must reset a software counter every PID cycle
     - A separate low-priority task monitors this counter
     - If counter not reset within 500 ms, trigger safe shutdown

  3. SENSOR WATCHDOG (Required):
     - Monitor heading data ($HDM) arrival rate
     - If no heading data for > 1.0 seconds:
       a. Flash warning on display
       b. DO NOT disengage (boat is still controllable, just no update)
       c. After 3.0 seconds with no heading data: DISENGAGE autopilot
       d. Log error: "COMPASS TIMEOUT"

  4. GPS WATCHDOG (for track mode):
     - If no GPS data for > 5.0 seconds in track mode:
       a. Flash warning: "GPS LOST"
       b. Automatically switch to HEADING HOLD mode (hold last heading)
       c. DO NOT disengage (heading hold still works without GPS)

  5. HEARTBEAT OUTPUT (Recommended):
     - Autopilot outputs a periodic heartbeat signal (e.g., toggling a GPIO
       pin at 1 Hz)
     - External monitor (or secondary processor) watches this heartbeat
     - Missing 3 heartbeats -> external solenoid cutoff relay opens

5.2 SOLENOID ENGAGEMENT TIMEOUT
--------------------------------

PURPOSE: Prevent the rudder from being driven continuously in one direction.
If the autopilot commands "hard to starboard" for 60 seconds, the boat will
enter a continuous turn, potentially gybing (sailboat) or circling.

REQUIREMENTS:

  Maximum continuous solenoid engagement (single direction):
    DEFAULT:     10 seconds
    USER RANGE:  5 - 30 seconds (adjustable)
    ABSOLUTE MAX: 30 seconds (not user-adjustable, hardware enforced)

  After timeout:
    1. De-energize the active solenoid (rudder returns to center, or holds
       position if hydraulically locked)
    2. Wait for center_deadband_time (1-3 seconds)
    3. Re-evaluate heading error
    4. If still needs correction, re-engage for another period

  This creates a "pulse and coast" behavior for large corrections:
    - Large heading error: 10 sec drive -> 2 sec coast -> 10 sec drive -> ...
    - Small heading error: Short pulses (<2 sec) with coast between

  IMPLEMENTATION:

    solenoid_active_time = 0
    SOLENOID_TIMEOUT = 10.0  // seconds

    in PID loop:
      if solenoid_active:
          solenoid_active_time += dt
          if solenoid_active_time > SOLENOID_TIMEOUT:
              de-energize_solenoid()
              solenoid_active = false
              solenoid_active_time = 0
              coast_timer = 2.0  // seconds to wait
      else:
          if coast_timer > 0:
              coast_timer -= dt
          elif |e_norm| > deadband:
              energize_solenoid(direction)
              solenoid_active = true
              solenoid_active_time = 0

5.3 RUDDER LIMIT DETECTION
---------------------------

PURPOSE: Detect when the rudder has reached its mechanical travel limit to
prevent hydraulic pump damage and detect mechanical faults.

METHODS (use at least two):

  METHOD 1: Rudder feedback sensor (preferred)
    - Linear potentiometer or Hall-effect sensor on rudder stock
    - Reports actual rudder angle (0-359 degrees or +/- 45 degrees)
    - If |rudder_angle_actual| > delta_max: de-energize drive solenoid
    - If rudder_angle_actual != rudder_angle_commanded for > 3 seconds:
      alarm "RUDDER FAULT"

  METHOD 2: Hydraulic pressure sensor
    - Pressure transducer on hydraulic supply line
    - When rudder hits mechanical stop, pressure spikes (2-3x normal)
    - Threshold: 500 - 1500 psi (system dependent)
    - On pressure spike: de-energize drive solenoid immediately

  METHOD 3: Pump current monitoring
    - Monitor current to hydraulic pump motor
    - Stall current (rudder at stop) is 2-4x running current
    - On overcurrent: de-energize after 0.5 sec delay (avoid false trips
      from normal pressure)

  METHOD 4: Software angle tracking (basic, insufficient alone)
    - Track commanded rudder angle over time
    - If command is at limit for > T_max_drive: assume limit reached
    - This is the LEAST reliable method (no feedback)

  REQUIRED IMPLEMENTATION:
    - Rudder feedback sensor + at least one other method
    - Alarm if any two methods disagree

5.4 OFF-COURSE ALARM THRESHOLDS
--------------------------------

PURPOSE: Alert the crew if the autopilot is failing to maintain course.

ALARM LEVELS:

  LEVEL 1 — CAUTION (audible beep, yellow indicator):
    Condition: |heading_error| > HEADING_WARN_DEGREES for > T_WARN seconds
    Typical:   10 degrees for > 15 seconds
    Action:     Display heading error, suggest manual check

  LEVEL 2 — WARNING (continuous beep, red indicator):
    Condition: |heading_error| > HEADING_ALARM_DEGREES for > T_ALARM seconds
    Typical:   20 degrees for > 30 seconds
    Action:     Display "OFF COURSE" alarm, suggest disengaging AP

  LEVEL 3 — CRITICAL (siren, flashing red, auto-disengage):
    Condition: |heading_error| > HEADING_CRITICAL_DEGREES for > T_CRIT seconds
    Typical:   45 degrees for > 10 seconds
    Action:    AUTO-DISENGAGE autopilot, alarm "AUTOPILOT DISENGAGED — OFF COURSE"

  XTE ALARM (track mode only):
    Condition: |XTE| > XTE_ALARM_NM
    Coastal:   0.1 nm (185 m) — immediate alarm
    Offshore:  0.5 nm (926 m) — alarm after 30 sec
    Ocean:     1.0 nm (1852 m) — alarm after 60 sec

5.5 MANUAL OVERRIDE — ALWAYS WORKS
-----------------------------------

ABSOLUTE REQUIREMENT: The helmsman must be able to override the autopilot
at ALL times, regardless of software state, processor state, or power state.

IMPLEMENTATION:

  1. MECHANICAL CLUTCH (Required, physical disconnect):
     - A lever or rotary handle that physically disconnects the autopilot
       drive from the rudder stock
     - When clutch is disengaged, the wheel/tiller is directly connected to
       the rudder with NO electrical intervention
     - Clutch must be operable with ONE HAND
     - Clutch position must be VISIBLE (indicator marking)
     - Clutch must be MECHANICAL (not electrical)
     - Clutch engagement should require positive action (spring to disengage)

  2. STANDBY BUTTON (Required):
     - A dedicated, physically prominent button marked "STANDBY" or "AUTO"
     - Momentary press toggles between autopilot engaged and standby
     - Button must be lit when autopilot is engaged
     - Button must be large enough to press with gloves
     - Button must work regardless of display state
     - MUST be hardwired to the processor (not via touchscreen)

  3. EMERGENCY DISENGAGE (Recommended):
     - A mushroom-head emergency stop button
     - Physically cuts power to solenoid driver circuits
     - Independent of processor (direct relay contact)
     - Latching — must be physically reset to re-enable

5.6 POWER FAILURE BEHAVIOR
---------------------------

RULE: On power loss, the autopilot MUST fail to a safe state.

  HYDRAULIC SYSTEMS:
    - Solenoid valves are NORMALLY CLOSED (spring return to center)
    - On power loss, solenoids de-energize, hydraulic fluid flows to center
    - Rudder returns to center position (or drifts to center via autopump)
    - Some systems use a mechanical centering spring on the rudder

  ELECTRICAL SYSTEMS:
    - All solenoid driver outputs are open-drain (or use normally-open relays)
    - Power loss = all outputs LOW = all solenoids de-energized

  BATTERY BACKUP (Recommended):
    - Small backup battery (e.g., 12V 7Ah lead-acid) for the control electronics
    - Provides 5-15 minutes of control after main power loss
    - Allows orderly autopilot disengagement and alarm sounding
    - Does NOT drive the solenoids (too much current draw)
    - Purpose: Keep the microcontroller alive long enough to:
      1. Sound alarm
      2. Log the power failure event
      3. Ensure solenoid driver outputs are LOW
      4. Display "POWER FAILURE" message

  POWER MONITORING:
    - Continuously monitor supply voltage
    - If V_supply < 10.0V (12V system) or < 20.0V (24V system):
      a. Alarm: "LOW VOLTAGE"
      b. Reduce autopilot gain (less aggressive steering to save power)
      c. If V_supply < 9.0V (12V) or < 18.0V (24V):
         AUTO-DISENGAGE autopilot ("VOLTAGE TOO LOW")

5.7 SEA STATE COMPENSATION
---------------------------

PROBLEM: In rough seas, wave-induced heading oscillations cause the PID
controller to fight the waves, resulting in:
  - Excessive rudder movement (hydraulic pump cycling, power waste, heat)
  - Poor course holding (the AP may actually make things worse)
  - Uncomfortable ride (boat may yaw more with AP than without)

SOLUTION: Reduce PID gains and increase deadband in rough conditions.

SEA STATE DETECTION:
  METHOD 1: Manual sea state setting (user selects 1-9 on AP display)
  METHOD 2: Automatic detection from heading variance:
    - Compute rolling standard deviation of heading error over 30-second window
    - sigma_heading > 3 deg: Sea State >= 3 (moderate)
    - sigma_heading > 6 deg: Sea State >= 4 (rough)
    - sigma_heading > 10 deg: Sea State >= 5 (very rough)
  METHOD 3: Automatic detection from rate of turn (if $ROT available)

GAIN SCHEDULE vs SEA STATE:

  | Sea State | Description  | Gain Factor | Deadband Multiplier |
  |-----------|-------------|-------------|---------------------|
  | 0-2       | Calm        | 1.00        | 1.0x                |
  | 3         | Moderate    | 0.75        | 1.5x                |
  | 4         | Rough       | 0.50        | 2.0x                |
  | 5         | Very rough  | 0.35        | 3.0x                |
  | 6+        | Severe      | 0.20        | 5.0x                |
  | DISENGAGE | Extreme     | —           | —                   |

  Implementation:
    Kp_eff = Kp * gain_factor * speed_factor * seastate_factor
    Ki_eff = Ki * gain_factor * speed_factor * seastate_factor
    Kd_eff = Kd * speed_factor                  // Kd often NOT reduced
    db_eff = db_base * db_multiplier

  NOTE: In Sea State 6+, the autopilot should recommend disengagement.
  In Sea State 8+, the autopilot should auto-disengage with alarm:
  "SEA STATE TOO HIGH — AUTOPILOT DISENGAGED"

  RATIONALE for reducing Kd less (or not at all):
  - In rough seas, damping is MORE important (boat is more underdamped due
    to wave excitation)
  - Reducing Kd would increase oscillation amplitude
  - Kd reduction should only happen in extreme conditions (Sea State 6+)

SECTION 6: PID GAIN RANGES FOR DIFFERENT VESSEL TYPES
================================================================================

6.1 GAIN TABLE — NOMINAL VALUES AT REFERENCE CONDITIONS
--------------------------------------------------------

Reference conditions:
  - Boat speed: 5 knots through water
  - Sea State: 0-2 (calm to slight)
  - Loading: Half load, normal trim
  - Wind: < 10 kts
  - Units: [deg rudder per deg heading error] for Kp and Kd
           [deg rudder per (deg heading error * sec)] for Ki

  VESSEL TYPE                 | Kp     | Ki      | Kd     | Notes
  ----------------------------|--------|---------|--------|-----------------
  25ft sailboat               | 1.0-   | 0.01-   | 3.0-   | Light disp,
  (till., tiller steer)       | 2.0    | 0.05    | 8.0    | fast response
                              |        |         |        |
  35ft sailboat               | 1.5-   | 0.02-   | 5.0-   | Medium disp,
  (wheel, hydraulic)          | 3.0    | 0.08    | 12.0   | most common
                              |        |         |        |
  40ft powerboat              | 2.0-   | 0.03-   | 3.0-   | Heavy disp,
  (hydraulic, single engine)  | 4.0    | 0.10    | 8.0    | slower response
                              |        |         |        |
  50ft sailboat               | 1.0-   | 0.01-   | 5.0-   | Heavy disp,
  (hydraulic, wheel)          | 2.5    | 0.06    | 15.0   | high inertia
                              |        |         |        |

  STARTING POINTS FOR MANUAL TUNING:
    - Start with Kp at the LOWER end of the range
    - Ki = 0.01 * Kp (initial estimate)
    - Kd = 3.0 * Kp (initial estimate)
    - Increase Kp until oscillation begins, then back off 30%
    - Increase Kd to damp remaining oscillation
    - Increase Ki slowly to eliminate steady-state error

6.2 SPEED DEPENDENCE
---------------------

All gains should be scaled with the square of boat speed relative to the
tuning reference speed (5 knots):

  GAIN_SPEED_FACTOR = (V_STW / 5.0)^2

  HOWEVER, this factor should be CLAMPED:
    GAIN_SPEED_FACTOR = clamp(GAIN_SPEED_FACTOR, 0.15, 4.0)

  This prevents:
    - Gains going to zero at very low speeds (boat would be uncontrollable)
    - Gains becoming dangerously high at planing speeds

  FULL TABLE — 35FT SAILBOAT GAINS vs SPEED:

  | Speed (kts) | Speed Factor | Kp        | Ki        | Kd        |
  |-------------|-------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
  | 2           | 0.16        | 0.3-0.5   | 0.003-0.013| 5.0-12.0 |
  | 3           | 0.36        | 0.5-1.1   | 0.007-0.029| 5.0-12.0 |
  | 5           | 1.00        | 1.5-3.0   | 0.02-0.08 | 5.0-12.0 |
  | 7           | 1.96        | 2.9-5.9   | 0.039-0.157| 5.0-12.0 |
  | 10          | 4.00(clamp) | 6.0-12.0  | 0.08-0.32 | 5.0-12.0 |
  | 15          | 4.00(clamp) | 6.0-12.0  | 0.08-0.32 | 5.0-12.0 |

  NOTE: Kd is NOT scaled by speed in the above table. This is intentional:
  - At low speed, the boat responds slowly and needs MORE damping relative
    to the proportional gain to prevent overshoot
  - At high speed, the rudder is very effective and Kd should not increase
    (it would amplify sensor noise into aggressive rudder movements)
  - Some implementations DO scale Kd by speed factor^0.5 as a compromise

  ALTERNATIVE (simpler, recommended for first implementation):
    - Scale Kp and Ki by speed^2 as shown
    - Keep Kd CONSTANT across speed range
    - This provides good stability across the speed range

6.3 SEA STATE DEPENDENCE
-------------------------

Gains are reduced and deadband increased with sea state:

  FULL TABLE — 35FT SAILBOAT GAINS vs SEA STATE (at 5 kts):

  | Sea State | Gain Factor | Deadband | Kp        | Ki        | Kd        |
  |-----------|-------------|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
  | 0-2       | 1.00        | 1.0 deg  | 1.5-3.0   | 0.02-0.08 | 5.0-12.0 |
  | 3         | 0.75        | 1.5 deg  | 1.1-2.3   | 0.015-0.06| 5.0-12.0 |
  | 4         | 0.50        | 2.0 deg  | 0.75-1.5  | 0.01-0.04 | 5.0-12.0 |
  | 5         | 0.35        | 3.0 deg  | 0.5-1.1   | 0.007-0.028|5.0-12.0 |

  Note: In Sea State 5, the effective Kp drops to 0.5-1.1. The boat will
  wander more, but will not fight the waves. This is the correct tradeoff:
  a +/- 10 degree heading weave in Sea State 5 is acceptable and expected.
  Aggressive correction would make the ride worse.

6.4 COMBINED GAIN SCHEDULE
---------------------------

The effective gains combine speed and sea state factors:

  Kp_eff = Kp_nominal * SPEED_FACTOR * SEASTATE_FACTOR
  Ki_eff = Ki_nominal * SPEED_FACTOR * SEASTATE_FACTOR
  Kd_eff = Kd_nominal * 1.0  (no sea state scaling for Kd)

  Where:
    SPEED_FACTOR  = clamp((V_STW / V_ref)^2, 0.15, 4.0)
    SEASTATE_FACTOR = lookup from sea state table

  DEADBAND:
    db_eff = db_base * SEASTATE_DB_MULTIPLIER

6.5 TUNING PROCEDURE (FIELD PROCEDURE)
--------------------------------------

STEP 1: PREPARATION
  - Choose a calm day (Sea State 0-2) with light wind (< 10 kts)
  - Motor at 5 kts in open water (at least 1 nm from hazards)
  - Ensure compass is calibrated and $HDM data is flowing
  - Ensure rudder feedback is calibrated (center = 0 deg)

STEP 2: ENGAGE AND SET INITIAL GAINS
  - Set Kp = 1.5, Ki = 0.02, Kd = 5.0 (starting point for 35ft sailboat)
  - Engage autopilot in HEADING HOLD mode
  - Set heading to current heading (zero error)

STEP 3: TUNE Kp
  - Command a 20-degree heading change
  - Observe response:
    - Too slow (>30 sec to reach new heading): Increase Kp by 25%
    - Oversoot > 10 deg: Decrease Kp by 20%
    - Oscillation (more than 2 cycles): Decrease Kp by 30%
    - 1-2 deg overshoot with smooth settling: Kp is correct
  - Repeat until response is satisfactory

STEP 4: TUNE Kd
  - With Kp set, command 30-degree heading changes
  - Observe overshoot and oscillation:
    - If overshoot > 5 deg: Increase Kd by 25%
    - If rudder is "chattering" (rapid small corrections): Decrease Kd by 20%
    - If heading settles in one smooth pass with < 3 deg overshoot: Kd correct
  - IMPORTANT: Kd amplifies compass noise. If the compass is noisy (> 2 deg
    noise), Kd may need to be lower than theoretically optimal.

STEP 5: TUNE Ki
  - Sail on a steady heading for 5+ minutes in a steady breeze
  - Observe steady-state heading error:
    - If error drifts > 3 deg from wind: Increase Ki by 50%
    - If heading oscillates slowly (period > 60 sec): Decrease Ki by 50%
    - Ki should be SMALL. Too much Ki causes slow oscillation (period 2-5 min)
  - Start with Ki = 0.01 and increase VERY slowly

STEP 6: VERIFY AT DIFFERENT SPEEDS
  - Repeat heading changes at 3 kts and 7 kts
  - If response changes significantly, adjust gain scheduling
  - The speed^2 scaling should handle most of this automatically

STEP 7: VERIFY IN ROUGH WATER
  - On a day with Sea State 3-4, repeat tests
  - If excessive rudder activity: Reduce seastate gain factor
  - Increase deadband if rudder is "hunting"

STEP 8: DOCUMENT AND SAVE
  - Record final Kp, Ki, Kd, deadband, speed factor, sea state table
  - Save to non-volatile memory
  - Label: "TUNED: [DATE], CONDITIONS: [describe], VESSEL: [name]"

APPENDIX A: TRANSFER FUNCTION DERIVATION
================================================================================

The Nomoto model is the standard simplified model for vessel steering:

  T1 * T2 * r_dot + (T1 + T2) * r + K * delta = 0

  Where:
    r     = yaw rate (d(psi)/dt) [rad/s]
    delta = rudder angle [rad]
    T1, T2 = time constants [sec]
    K     = gain [1/sec]

  For many vessels, T2 is small and the model reduces to first-order:

    T * r_dot + r = -K * delta    (NOMOTO FIRST-ORDER MODEL)

  Converting to heading:
    r = d(psi)/dt
    T * psi_ddot + psi_dot = -K * delta

  Taking the Laplace transform (zero initial conditions):

    s * (T*s + 1) * Psi(s) = -K * Delta(s)

    Psi(s)       -K
    --------- = ---------
    Delta(s)     s*(T*s + 1)

  This is a type-1 system (integrator + first-order lag).

  For the second-order model (retaining T2):

    Psi(s)             -K
    --------- = -------------------
    Delta(s)     s*(T1*s + 1)*(T2*s + 1)

  TYPICAL VALUES (35ft sailboat):

    K  = 0.05 - 0.15  [1/sec]
    T1 = 5 - 15       [sec]
    T2 = 0.5 - 3.0    [sec]

  CONVERTING TO HEADING ERROR TRANSFER FUNCTION:

    Error = Heading_command - Heading_actual

    E(s)           s*(T*s + 1)
    ----- = 1 - (-K / (s*(T*s+1))) = 1 + K/(s*(T*s+1)) = ----------------
    Delta(s)                                           s*(T*s+1) + K

    Open-loop transfer function:
    L(s) = Kp * (-K) / (s*(T*s + 1))

    (The negative sign accounts for the fact that positive rudder angle
     produces a negative heading rate change — i.e., the rudder turns the
     boat, and the sign convention matters.)

    For stability analysis, we analyze:
    L(s) = Kp * K / (s*(T*s + 1))

    Gain crossover frequency (where |L(jw)| = 1):
    wc = sqrt(Kp * K / T)

    Phase margin:
    PM = 90 - atan(wc * T) degrees

    For adequate stability, PM > 45 degrees is required.

APPENDIX B: NMEA SENTENCE WIRE PROTOCOL REFERENCE
================================================================================

NMEA 0183 SENTENCE STRUCTURE:

  $<talker><sentence>,<field1>,<field2>,...*<checksum><CR><LF>

  COMPONENTS:
    $        = Start of sentence delimiter
    talker   = 2-character talker ID (e.g., GP, HD, AG)
    sentence = 3-character sentence ID (e.g., HDM, APB, RMB)
    ,        = Field separator
    fieldN   = Data field (comma-delimited, may be empty for missing data)
    *        = Checksum delimiter
    checksum = 2-character hexadecimal checksum
    <CR><LF> = Carriage return + Line feed (0x0D 0x0A)

  CHECKSUM CALCULATION:
    XOR all characters between '$' and '*' (exclusive of both)
    Output as 2-digit uppercase hex

  Example: $HDHDM,045.5,M
    XOR of: H, D, H, D, M, ',', 0, 4, 5, '.', 5, ',', M
    = 0x48 ^ 0x44 ^ 0x48 ^ 0x44 ^ 0x4D ^ 0x2C ^ 0x30 ^ 0x34 ^ 0x35 ^
      0x2E ^ 0x35 ^ 0x2C ^ 0x4D
    = 0x2A
    Full sentence: $HDHDM,045.5,M*2A

  RS-422 ELECTRICAL LEVELS:
    TX+ (A): V_OH = +5V, V_OL = 0V  (relative to TX-)
    TX- (B): V_OH = 0V,  V_OL = +5V  (complement)
    RX threshold: |V_diff| > 200mV = valid
    Common mode range: -7V to +12V

  MAXIMUM BUS LENGTH:  100 meters at 4800 baud
  MAXIMUM NODES:       1 talker + 10 listeners (at 4800 baud)
  TERMINATION:         120-ohm resistor at each end of bus

APPENDIX C: ANTI-WINDUP ALGORITHM PSEUDOCODE
================================================================================

```
// CONSTANTS
INT_MAX       = 700.0     // Maximum integral accumulator (deg * sec)
E_INT_THRESH  = 15.0      // Integral enable threshold (deg)
T_INT_LOCKOUT = 5.0       // Lockout after engage/reset (sec)
INT_DECAY     = 0.0001    // Integral decay rate (1/sec)

// STATE
int_accum     = 0.0
t_since_reset = 0.0

FUNCTION update_integral(e_norm, dt, rudder_at_limit, mode_changed):
    if mode_changed:
        int_accum = 0.0
        t_since_reset = 0.0
        return 0.0

    t_since_reset += dt

    // CONDITIONAL INTEGRATION: only accumulate when appropriate
    accumulate = true

    if |e_norm| > E_INT_THRESH:
        accumulate = false  // Error too large (course change, etc.)

    if t_since_reset < T_INT_LOCKOUT:
        accumulate = false  // Just engaged or reset

    if rudder_at_limit:
        accumulate = false  // Rudder saturated, no point accumulating

    if accumulate:
        int_accum += e_norm * dt

    // SLOW DECAY: prevent indefinite accumulation
    int_accum *= (1.0 - INT_DECAY * dt)

    // CLAMP: prevent integral from commanding beyond rudder limits
    int_accum = clamp(int_accum, -INT_MAX, +INT_MAX)

    return Ki * int_accum
```

APPENDIX D: SOLENOID DRIVER CIRCUIT NOTES
================================================================================

TYPICAL HYDRAULIC SOLENOID SPECIFICATIONS:
  Voltage:           12V DC (or 24V DC for commercial vessels)
  Current:           1.0 - 2.5 A per solenoid (pull-in)
  Hold current:      0.5 - 1.5 A per solenoid (after pull-in)
  Activation time:   20 - 50 ms
  Deactivation time: 30 - 80 ms (spring return)

DRIVER CIRCUIT REQUIREMENTS:
  1. MOSFET or relay driver rated for 3A continuous, 10A surge
  2. Flyback diode (1N4007 or equivalent) across each solenoid coil
  3. Output must be OPEN-DRAIN (fails to OFF on power loss)
  4. Separate PORT and STARBOARD solenoid outputs (3-wire: port, starboard, common)
  5. NEVER energize both solenoids simultaneously (dead zone)
  6. Minimum inter-solenoid dead time: 50 ms

  Protection:
  - Overcurrent protection: 3A fuse per solenoid
  - Thermal protection: heatsink on MOSFETs
  - Reverse polarity protection on supply input
  - EMC filtering: 100uF + 100nF capacitors on solenoid supply

  MICROCONTROLLER INTERFACE:
  - Two GPIO outputs: PORT_SOL and STBD_SOL
  - Both LOW = rudder centered (no drive)
  - PORT_SOL HIGH = rudder drives to port
  - STBD_SOL HIGH = rudder drives to starboard
  - Software must ensure mutual exclusion (never both HIGH)

  PUMP CONTROL (if applicable):
  - Some systems have a constant-pressure hydraulic pump (always running)
  - Some systems have an on-demand pump (activated with solenoid)
  - If on-demand: PUMP_ENABLE signal must lead solenoid by 100-200 ms
    to build pressure before the solenoid opens the valve
