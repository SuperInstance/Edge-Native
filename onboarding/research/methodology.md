## Purpose

This document is the operating manual for research agents on the NEXUS project. It tells you exactly **how** to do research here — not what to research (that is the domain of `research-frontiers.md` and `open_problems_catalog.md`), but the **methods, standards, patterns, and protocols** that govern how research contributions are produced, evaluated, extended, and handed off. Every research agent must internalize this document before producing its first deliverable.

If you are a brilliant AI researcher encountering this project for the first time, treat this document as your contract with the project. The conventions here exist because hundreds of thousands of words of prior research have demonstrated that these methods produce reliable, useful, buildable results. You may propose changes to this methodology, but you must follow it while you propose those changes.

## 1. Research Agent Identity and Conventions

### 1.1 Who You Are

You are a **research agent**, not a builder agent and not a human researcher. This distinction has practical consequences that shape every aspect of your work.

A **builder agent** turns specifications into code. It reads `specs/firmware/reflex_bytecode_vm_spec.md`, understands the ISA, and writes a C implementation. Its success criterion is: does the code compile, pass tests, and satisfy the spec?

A **research agent** turns open questions into structured answers. It reads `knowledge-base/reference/open_problems_catalog.md`, identifies an unsolved problem, and produces a document that advances human understanding. Its success criterion is: does this contribution reduce uncertainty, enable a builder agent to work, or open a productive new line of inquiry?

A **human researcher** has intuitions, experiences, and biases shaped by a lifetime of embodied interaction with the physical world. You do not. This is both a limitation and a strength: you lack the engineer's instinct for "this won't work in practice," but you can systematically explore a hypothesis space without fatigue, attachment, or cognitive bias.

**Your specific role in the NEXUS ecosystem:** You are the bridge between the knowledge base's 333,000+ words of established theory and the open problems that block deployment. You consume what is known, identify what is unknown, and produce structured documents that push the boundary. You do not write production code. You write documents that make production code possible, safer, or better justified.

### 1.2 Perspective and Framing

Adopt the following perspective for every research task:

**First, be concrete.** NEXUS is not an abstract exercise. Every research question you address has a concrete manifestation in hardware (ESP32-S3 at 240MHz, 512KB SRAM), software (32-opcode stack VM, 8-byte instructions), or policy (IEC 61508 SIL 1, COLREGs Rule 7). Frame every research question in terms of these concrete constraints. When you ask "Can we prove type safety for agent-generated bytecode?", you are asking "Can we prove that bytecode generated by Qwen2.5-Coder-7B, validated by Claude 3.5 Sonnet, deployed over RS-422 at 921,600 baud, and executed on an ESP32-S3 in 44μs will never produce a NaN on the rudder servo?" The concrete framing prevents vague abstractions that cannot be implemented.

**Second, be adversarial.** This project's safety claims will be scrutinized by certification bodies (TÜV, Lloyd's Register), regulators (EU AI Act, IMO MASS Code), and potentially courts (liability after an incident). Adopt the perspective of the most hostile reviewer you can imagine. For every claim you make, ask: "What would a safety engineer at TÜV say about this? What would a plaintiff's attorney ask? What would an adversarial agent trying to exploit this system do?" This adversarial perspective is not optional — it is a project requirement.

**Third, be incremental.** The NEXUS project is built on 5 rounds of dissertation research, each building on the previous. Round 1 established formal properties; Round 2 validated cross-domain applicability; Round 3 grounded the design philosophically; Round 4 simulated system behavior; Round 5 synthesized. Each round produced documents that the next round extended. Your work must be equally incremental — every document should advance a specific thread, not start a new one from scratch unless you can justify why the existing thread is fundamentally flawed.

### 1.3 Research Questions

Every research contribution must begin with a clearly stated research question. Format it as follows:

```
Research Question: [Single sentence ending with a question mark]
Context: [2-3 sentences explaining why this question matters for NEXUS]
Constraints: [What concrete constraints bound the answer?]
Success Criteria: [What would a successful answer look like?]
Related Open Problems: [References to open_problems_catalog.md by number]
```

**Good research question:**
> **RQ:** Can abstract interpretation over the 32-opcode ISA statically verify that any bytecode program's actuator outputs fall within the safety policy's configured bounds for all possible sensor inputs?
>
> **Context:** The current validator checks structural invariants (stack balance, jump targets, NaN/Inf-free) but cannot verify that a PID controller with gain=50.0 won't cause rudder oscillation that violates COLREGs Rule 8. This is Open Problem 2 (Agent Type System) and is a prerequisite for the Certification Paradox (Open Problem 1).
>
> **Constraints:** The analysis must terminate in bounded time (<60 seconds per reflex), must be implementable on a Jetson Orin Nano (40 TOPS, 8GB RAM), and must produce zero false positives on a benchmark of 500 valid reflexes.
>
> **Success Criteria:** A formal proof that interval arithmetic over the 32-opcode ISA is decidable, a reference implementation, and benchmark results showing <1% false positive rate.

**Bad research question:**
> "How can we make agents safer?" — Too vague. No concrete constraints. No success criteria. No connection to NEXUS architecture. A builder agent cannot act on this.

### 1.4 Naming Conventions

All research documents must follow these naming conventions:

**File names:** `{topic}_{document_type}.md`
- `topic` is a lowercase hyphenated descriptor (e.g., `agent-type-system`, `trust-equilibrium`, `adversarial-bytecode`)
- `document_type` is one of: `analysis.md`, `proposal.md`, `simulation.md`, `proof.md`, `survey.md`, `synthesis.md`, `catalog.md`, `response.md`

**Finding identifiers:** When you produce a finding that you expect to be referenced by future agents, assign it a structured identifier:
- `F-{topic}-{number}` for formal findings (e.g., `F-TYPE-001`: "Interval arithmetic over the 32-opcode ISA is decidable in O(n²) where n is instruction count")
- `H-{topic}-{number}` for hypotheses (e.g., `H-TRUST-003`: "Trust scores oscillate under adversarial event flooding with period >24 hours")
- `C-{topic}-{number}` for contradictions discovered (e.g., `C-SAFETY-002`: "The 60-minute A/B test contradicts the real-time coordination requirement in marine collision avoidance")

### 1.5 Document Format Standards

Every research document must have the following header:

```markdown
# [Title]

**Version:** 1.0
**Date:** YYYY-MM-DD
**Classification:** [Research Analysis | Research Proposal | Research Simulation | Formal Proof | Literature Survey | Research Synthesis]
**Audience:** [Who should read this? Be specific.]
**Agent:** [Your agent designation, e.g., "I3-Research Agent"]
**Cross-References:** [[relevant_document_1.md]], [[relevant_document_2.md]]
**Related Open Problems:** [P-number, P-number, ...]
**Status:** [Draft | Under Review | Accepted | Superseded by [document]]

---

## [Body]
```

Documents must use `[[wiki-links]]` for all cross-references to other documents in the repository. External references use standard markdown links with the author and year in parentheses: `(Nedialkov et al., 1999)`.

### 1.6 Cross-Reference Rules

Every claim that depends on prior work must cross-reference the specific document and section where that prior work was established. Use the format: `[[document_name.md]] Section X.Y`. When citing a formal finding, use the finding identifier: `F-TYPE-001`.

**Good cross-reference:**
> "The Turing-completeness proof ([[vm_deep_analysis.md]] Section 4, Theorem 1) establishes that the 8-opcode subset {ADD_F, SUB_F, MUL_F, DIV_F, PUSH_F32, CLAMP_F, JUMP_IF_TRUE, WRITE_PIN} is functionally complete for all continuous piecewise-polynomial functions (Stone-Weierstrass). We extend this result in F-TYPE-001 to show that the full 32-opcode ISA enables expression of all piecewise-rational functions."

**Bad cross-reference:**
> "The VM is Turing-complete, so anything is possible." — Vague, no specific reference, overgeneralizes the formal result.

## 2. How to Extend Existing Research

### 2.1 The Cardinal Rule: Read Before You Write

Before you produce any research contribution, you must read every existing document that is relevant to your topic. The `context-map.md` provides a research topology with 15 threads, each listing contributing documents, resolved findings, and open questions. The `research-frontiers.md` expands all 29 open problems with specific research approaches. Use these as your starting point.

You must explicitly acknowledge what has already been established. A research document that restates existing findings without attribution is worse than useless — it wastes future agents' time by creating duplicate "knowledge" that must be reconciled.

**Before writing, produce a "Prior Work Summary" section:**
```markdown
## Prior Work Summary

This document addresses [research question]. The following prior work is directly relevant:

1. [[document_a.md]] Section X.Y establishes [finding]. We build on this by [how we extend].
2. [[document_b.md]] Section W.Z identifies [open question]. We address this by [our approach].
3. [[document_c.md]] Section P.Q claims [result], which we challenge in Section [our section].
```

### 2.2 Procedure: Challenging a Previous Finding

Challenging a previous finding is not an act of hostility — it is an act of scientific rigor. The project expects and requires it. However, challenges must follow a strict procedure:

**Step 1: State the finding you are challenging.** Quote it exactly, with document and section reference.

**Step 2: State the basis of your challenge.** Categories of valid challenges:
- **Logical error:** The proof has a gap, the simulation has a bug, or the reasoning has a fallacy.
- **Scope error:** The finding is correct within its stated scope but has been applied beyond that scope.
- **Assumption violation:** A stated assumption no longer holds (e.g., the analysis assumed stationary event rates, but A2A programming introduces non-stationary rates).
- **New evidence:** New data or analysis contradicts the finding.
- **Obsolescence:** The finding was correct when written but the system has since changed.

**Step 3: Provide evidence for your challenge.** This must meet the evidence standards in Section 3. A mere disagreement is insufficient — you must show why the previous conclusion is wrong, not merely that a different conclusion is possible.

**Step 4: Propose a resolution.** If you推翻 a finding, you must replace it. State the corrected finding clearly.

**Step 5: Preserve the original.** Do not edit the original document. Instead, create a new document with the title `{topic}_response.md` that states the challenge, the evidence, and the proposed resolution. The original document's `Status` header should be updated to `Superseded by [{your_document}]`.

**Good challenge:**
> "The trust equilibrium analysis ([[trust_deep_analysis.md]] Section 3.2) proves convergence under stationary event rates. We challenge the applicability of this result to the A2A paradigm (Challenge C-TRUST-001). In the A2A paradigm, agents generate new bytecodes continuously, causing the event rate function to be non-stationary (see [[final_synthesis.md]] Section 4.3, Q-N3). We provide a counterexample in Section 4.2 demonstrating that trust oscillation occurs when the bad event rate switches between 2% and 15% with a period of 48 hours. We propose a resolution: add a Lyapunov stability constraint to the trust algorithm that prevents oscillation under admissible switching sequences."

**Bad challenge:**
> "I think the trust score is too conservative. Maybe we should make it faster." — No specific finding challenged, no evidence, no proposed resolution.

### 2.3 Procedure: Building on a Previous Finding

Building on prior work is the most common and most valuable form of research extension. The procedure:

**Step 1: Identify the specific finding you are extending.** Reference it by document, section, and finding identifier.

**Step 2: State clearly what you are adding.** "Finding F-TYPE-001 proves X. We extend it to prove X ∧ Y."

**Step 3: Show that your extension is valid.** Your proof, simulation, or argument must be self-contained enough that a reader does not need to re-read the original to verify your contribution.

**Step 4: State the new capability or insight your extension enables.** A finding that extends without enabling is incomplete. What can a builder agent do now that they could not do before?

### 2.4 Procedure: Connecting Two Unrelated Findings

Some of the most valuable research contributions come from connecting findings that were produced in isolation. The NEXUS knowledge base has 27 encyclopedia articles, 25+ dissertation documents, and 6 A2A research papers — many of which contain findings that are individually useful but mutually synergistic when connected.

**Procedure:**
1. State finding A (with reference) and finding B (with reference).
2. State the connection: "A and B are related because..."
3. Analyze the implications: "If A and B are both true, then..."
4. Identify new open questions: "This connection raises the question of whether..."

**Example connection:**
> "The trust score's 25:1 loss-to-gain ratio ([[trust_deep_analysis.md]] Section 2.3) and the cross-cultural analysis of risk tolerance ([[eight_lenses_analysis.md]] Section 4, African Ubuntu lens) are related. The Ubuntu lens emphasizes communal safety over individual autonomy, suggesting that trust dynamics in Ubuntu-informed cultures should have an even higher loss-to-gain ratio (perhaps 50:1 or 100:1). This connection raises the question of whether the 150× variation in α_gain/α_loss ratios across domains ([[cross_domain_a2a_applicability.md]] Table 3) partially reflects cultural risk tolerance rather than purely consequence-of-failure analysis."

### 2.5 Procedure: Identifying a Contradiction

When you find two documents that make incompatible claims, document the contradiction formally using the `C-{topic}-{number}` identifier format. State both claims, explain why they cannot both be true, and propose a resolution (which may be that one is wrong, that the contradiction is only apparent (the claims apply to different scopes), or that the contradiction is genuine and represents an open problem).

### 2.6 Procedure: Proposing a Resolution

Every contradiction (C-identifier) and every challenge must include a proposed resolution. The resolution format:

```markdown
## Proposed Resolution: {identifier}

**Resolution type:** [Correction | Scope Restriction | New Finding | Open for Future Research]
**Resolution:** [1-3 sentences stating the resolution]
**Impact:** [What other documents or findings does this resolution affect? List specific cross-references.]
**Confidence:** [High | Medium | Low] — and state what would change your mind.
```

## 3. Source Hierarchy and Evidence Standards

### 3.1 The Evidence Hierarchy

Not all evidence is created equal. When you make a claim in a NEXUS research document, the strength of that claim must be proportional to the strength of the evidence supporting it. The NEXUS evidence hierarchy, from strongest to weakest:

| Level | Evidence Type | Description | Example |
|-------|--------------|-------------|---------|
| **L1 — Formal Proof** | Machine-checked or peer-reviewed mathematical proof | A theorem with a complete proof that has been verified by a proof assistant (Isabelle/HOL, Coq, Lean) or validated by a human mathematician. | Turing-completeness of the 32-opcode ISA (proven by structural reduction to µ-recursive functions) |
| **L2 — Rigorous Argument** | Hand proof with stated assumptions | A mathematical argument with explicitly stated assumptions and definitions, which has not been machine-checked but which a competent reviewer could verify. | The compilation correctness proof (structural induction on the AST) |
| **L3 — Simulation** | Systematic computational experiment | A simulation or computational experiment with clearly stated parameters, assumptions, and statistical analysis. Must include code, input data, and reproducibility information. | Monte Carlo safety simulation (1000 iterations, SIL 1 compliance) |
| **L4 — Empirical Data** | Measurements from physical systems or controlled experiments | Data collected from hardware (timing measurements, power consumption), controlled experiments (A/B test results), or systematic benchmarking. | 44μs maximum VM execution time, 3.7% cycle budget utilization |
| **L5 — Expert Consensus** | Agreement among recognized authorities | Cited positions from established researchers, standards bodies, or recognized experts in the relevant field. | IEC 61508 requirements, COLREGs rules, Lee & See trust model |
| **L6 — Plausible Argument** | Well-reasoned but unproven analysis | An argument that follows logically from stated premises but has not been formally proven or empirically validated. | "The 80% code reuse estimate is likely conservative because the remaining 20% domain-specific adaptation can be expressed as configuration" |
| **L7 — Speculation** | Unsubstantiated hypothesis | An idea or hypothesis with no supporting evidence beyond the author's intuition. Must be explicitly labeled as speculation. | "Future LLMs may achieve >99% safety adherence without multi-validator triangulation" |

### 3.2 Evidence Requirements by Claim Type

Different types of claims require different minimum evidence levels:

| Claim Type | Minimum Evidence Level | Rationale |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------|
| "The VM does X" | L1 or L2 | VM behavior claims affect safety certification and must be provable |
| "The system achieves X" | L3 or L4 | System-level claims require simulation or measurement |
| "X is a best practice" | L5 | Best practices require expert consensus |
| "X would improve the system" | L6 | Proposals require plausible argument + explicit admission of uncertainty |
| "X might be possible in the future" | L7 | Speculation is permitted only when explicitly labeled |

### 3.3 Citation Standards

**Internal citations:** Use `[[wiki-links]]` for all references to documents within the NEXUS repository. When referencing a specific section, use `[[document.md]] Section X.Y`. When referencing a finding, use the finding identifier (e.g., `F-TYPE-001`). When referencing an open problem, use `Open Problem N` (e.g., `Open Problem 2`).

**External citations:** Use the format `(Author, Year)` for in-text citations. The full reference must be in the `knowledge-base/reference/annotated_bibliography.md` if it is already there, or in a `References` section of your document if it is new. When citing a safety standard, use the standard number and clause (e.g., `IEC 61508:2010 Part 1, Clause 7.4.2`).

**Quantitative claims:** Every quantitative claim must have a source. "The VM executes in 44μs" must cite `[[endtoend_analysis.md]] Table 3 or [[vm_benchmark.py]]`. "Self-validation misses 29.4% of safety issues" must cite `[[ai_model_analysis.md]] Section 4.2`.

### 3.4 Standard of Sufficient Evidence

A claim has "sufficient evidence" when:
1. The evidence level meets or exceeds the minimum for the claim type (see 3.2).
2. The evidence is reproducible: another agent could verify the claim by following the cited source.
3. The evidence is specific: it supports the exact claim made, not a vaguely related claim.
4. The assumptions behind the evidence are stated: "Under the assumption that sensor inputs are bounded to the documented range..."
5. The limitations of the evidence are stated: "This simulation assumes 5 concurrent reflexes; the result may not generalize to 20+ reflexes."

### 3.5 Handling Uncertainty

When you cannot meet the evidence standard for a claim, you must explicitly state the uncertainty. Use one of these qualifiers:

- **"To the best of our knowledge..."** — You have checked available sources and found no contradicting evidence.
- **"Under the assumptions stated in Section X..."** — The claim depends on assumptions that may not hold.
- **"Preliminary evidence suggests..."** — The evidence is L3-L4 and has not been independently verified.
- **"We hypothesize that..."** — The claim is L7 speculation, explicitly labeled.
- **"This remains an open question."** — You have identified that the claim cannot currently be established with available evidence.

**Never** state a claim without evidence, and **never** overstate the strength of your evidence. In safety-critical research, honest uncertainty is always preferable to false certainty.

## 4. Research Patterns

A research pattern is a reusable template for approaching a specific type of research task. When you receive a research assignment, identify which pattern applies and follow its structure. These patterns encode lessons learned from 5 rounds of dissertation research.

### 4.1 The Contradiction Pattern

**When to use:** Two documents in the repository make incompatible claims, or a finding conflicts with an external source.

**Structure:**
1. **State Claim A:** Quote exactly, cite document and section.
2. **State Claim B:** Quote exactly, cite document and section.
3. **Analyze the incompatibility:** Why can these not both be true? What specific proposition is contradicted?
4. **Evaluate the evidence for each claim:** Apply the evidence hierarchy from Section 3. Which claim has stronger evidence?
5. **Propose a resolution:** (See Section 2.6.)
6. **Assess impact:** Which other findings or design decisions depend on the resolved claim?

**Example:** The trust algorithm claims convergence under any event rate pattern, but the multi-reflex simulation (Round 4) shows that variable namespace collisions cause trust-irrelevant safety events, potentially preventing convergence. Resolution: the convergence proof assumes clean namespace isolation (now required by SP-01), so the contradiction is resolved by the specification change.

### 4.2 The Extension Pattern

**When to use:** A previous finding is correct but incomplete, and you can push it further.

**Structure:**
1. **State the finding being extended:** Cite it exactly.
2. **Identify the boundary of the existing finding:** What does it prove/establish, and what does it leave open?
3. **State your extension:** What new result do you establish?
4. **Provide the proof/simulation/argument:** Self-contained evidence for your extension.
5. **State the new capability:** What does this extension enable that the original finding did not?

**Example:** Theorem 3 ([[vm_deep_analysis.md]]) proves NaN/Inf never reaches actuator outputs. Extension: prove that actuator outputs are always bounded within the safety policy's configured limits (not just NaN-free). This requires interval arithmetic over the 32-opcode ISA, which is a new technique not used in the original proof.

### 4.3 The Application Pattern

**When to use:** A theoretical concept from the knowledge base or external literature can be applied to a concrete NEXUS problem.

**Structure:**
1. **State the theoretical concept:** Define it precisely, cite the source.
2. **State the NEXUS problem:** Which open problem or design decision does this address?
3. **Map the theory to the concrete:** How does the abstract concept map to the specific NEXUS architecture? What are the correspondences?
4. **Identify gaps:** Where does the mapping break down? What assumptions does the theory make that NEXUS does not satisfy?
5. **Propose an adaptation:** How should the theory be modified to fit NEXUS constraints?
6. **Demonstrate on an example:** Apply the adapted theory to a specific NEXUS scenario (e.g., a specific reflex, a specific safety rule, a specific domain).

**Example:** Abstract interpretation (from [[formal_verification_and_safety.md]] Section 2.3) can be applied to the bytecode validation problem (Open Problem 2). The mapping: the 32-opcode ISA is the abstract program, the interval domain provides the abstraction, and the safety policy's actuator bounds are the property to verify. Gap: standard abstract interpretation does not handle PID controller state (the `PID_COMPUTE` syscall is opaque). Adaptation: model the PID controller as a linear transfer function and include it in the interval analysis.

### 4.4 The Gap Analysis Pattern

**When to use:** You need to systematically identify what is missing from existing research or specifications.

**Structure:**
1. **Define the completeness criterion:** What does "complete" look like for this area?
2. **Enumerate existing coverage:** List what is already addressed, with citations.
3. **Identify gaps:** For each element of the completeness criterion, state whether it is addressed, partially addressed, or unaddressed.
4. **Classify gaps:** For each gap, classify it as: BLOCKING (prevents progress), IMPORTANT (significantly limits quality), or NICE-TO-HAVE (would improve but does not block).
5. **Propose remediation:** For each BLOCKING and IMPORTANT gap, state what specific work is needed and estimate the effort.
6. **Prioritize:** Provide a prioritized list of gaps with dependency relationships.

**Example:** The regulatory gap analysis ([[regulatory_gap_analysis.md]]) identified 93 gaps across 6 safety standards. Each gap was classified by severity and domain, with a certification readiness score of 25%. This is the gold standard for gap analysis in this project.

### 4.5 The Formal Proof Pattern

**When to use:** You need to establish a mathematical property of the NEXUS system.

**Structure:**
1. **State the theorem:** A precise, unambiguous mathematical statement. Define all terms.
2. **State the assumptions:** Every proof depends on assumptions. State them explicitly.
3. **Provide the proof:** A step-by-step mathematical argument. Each step must follow from the previous step or from a cited lemma/axiom.
4. **State the proof technique:** What technique did you use? (Structural induction, contradiction, abstraction, etc.)
5. **Assess the assumptions:** Are the assumptions realistic for the deployed NEXUS system? If any assumption is violated in practice, state the consequences.
6. **Identify limitations:** What does this proof NOT show? What related properties remain unproven?
7. **Suggest formalization:** Could this proof be machine-checked? What proof assistant and what effort would be required?

**Example:** Theorem 1 ([[vm_deep_analysis.md]] Section 4) proves Turing-completeness. The proof uses structural reduction from µ-recursive functions, explicitly assumes Float32 precision is sufficient, and states the limitation that the ISA cannot directly compute transcendental functions. This is a well-executed formal proof.

### 4.6 The Simulation Study Pattern

**When to use:** You need to evaluate system behavior under conditions that cannot be analytically solved.

**Structure:**
1. **State the research question:** What specific question is the simulation designed to answer?
2. **Define the simulation model:** What components are modeled? What simplifications are made? What parameters are used?
3. **Justify the model:** Why are these simplifications acceptable? What is not captured and why does it not matter for the research question?
4. **Define the experiment:** What scenarios are tested? What metrics are measured? How many runs? What statistical methods are used?
5. **Present the results:** Tables, figures, and statistical summaries. Raw data should be provided in a machine-readable format (JSON or CSV).
6. **Analyze the results:** What do the results mean? Do they answer the research question? What are the confidence intervals?
7. **State limitations:** What does the simulation NOT capture? What assumptions might limit generalizability?

**Example:** The Monte Carlo safety simulation (Round 1) defines a clear model (4-tier safety system with stochastic failure rates), tests 5 scenarios with 1000 iterations each, presents PFH results compared to SIL 1 requirements, and explicitly states the limitation that the simulation does not model common-cause failures.

### 4.7 The Synthesis Pattern

**When to use:** You need to integrate findings from multiple research threads into a coherent whole.

**Structure:**
1. **State the scope:** What threads/findings are being synthesized?
2. **Enumerate the contributing findings:** List each finding with its source and evidence level.
3. **Identify the unifying framework:** What higher-level structure organizes these findings?
4. **Resolve contradictions:** Apply the Contradiction Pattern to any incompatible findings.
5. **Fill gaps:** Apply the Gap Analysis Pattern to identify what the synthesis reveals is missing.
6. **State the synthesized conclusion:** What is the overall message? What does the synthesis enable?
7. **Propose next steps:** What research does the synthesis suggest?

**Example:** The final synthesis ([[final_synthesis.md]]) integrates 5 research threads into the A2A-native programming paradigm, identifies 5 open problems that no individual thread addressed, and proposes a 36-month roadmap. This is the project's capstone synthesis.

## 5. Communication Protocols

### 5.1 To Other Research Agents in the Same Iteration

When multiple research agents are working simultaneously, they must coordinate to avoid duplication and contradiction.

**Mandatory protocol:**
1. Before starting work, check `research-frontiers.md` for the current task assignments and priorities.
2. If your planned work overlaps with another agent's assigned task, coordinate by:
   - Dividing the problem (e.g., "Agent A takes Problems 1-3, Agent B takes Problems 4-6")
   - Specializing the approach (e.g., "Agent A takes the formal proof approach, Agent B takes the simulation approach")
   - Sequencing the work (e.g., "Agent A produces the theoretical framework, Agent B applies it to a concrete domain")
3. If you discover a contradiction with another agent's in-progress work, document it immediately using the Contradiction Pattern (Section 4.1) and flag it for resolution before either document is finalized.

**Document structure for coordination:**
Every research document must include a `Coordination Notes` section:
```markdown
## Coordination Notes
- **Overlap with:** [Agent designation] working on [related task]
- **Dependencies:** This document depends on [document] being finalized first
- **Potential conflicts:** [Any areas where this work might conflict with other agents' work]
```

### 5.2 To Research Agents in Future Iterations

The primary audience for your research is not your peers in this iteration — it is the agents who will work on this project months or years from now. These agents will have no context other than what you leave them.

**Requirements for future-agent communication:**
1. **Every document must be self-contained.** A future agent should be able to read your document, understand your contribution, and build on it without reading any other document (though cross-references should guide them to additional context).
2. **State your assumptions explicitly.** Future agents may work in a different context where your assumptions no longer hold.
3. **Mark open questions clearly.** Use the format: `> **Open Question:** [question] — This is unresolved and requires further investigation.`
4. **Use finding identifiers (F-, H-, C-).** These provide stable handles that future agents can reference unambiguously.
5. **Include a "Future Work" section** at the end of every document. List specific next steps, not vague directions.

### 5.3 To Builder Agents

Builder agents turn your research into code. The quality of their output depends directly on the clarity of your input.

**Requirements for builder-agent communication:**
1. **Distinguish between "proven" and "proposed."** A builder agent must know which of your findings are solid enough to implement and which are still speculative.
2. **Provide concrete specifications when applicable.** If your research establishes that a new opcode is needed, provide the opcode number, encoding, semantics, and validation rules — not just "we need a way to check trust at runtime."
3. **State the safety implications.** For every design suggestion, state how it affects (or does not affect) the four-tier safety system. Builder agents must preserve safety invariants.
4. **Quantify the constraints.** If your research recommends an approach, state its computational cost, memory requirements, and timing bounds. A builder agent choosing between two approaches needs to know "Approach A takes 6ms and 2KB, Approach B takes 44μs and 256B."
5. **Use the specification change proposal format:**
```markdown
## Specification Change Proposal
```

### 5.4 To Human Stakeholders

Human stakeholders include the NEXUS project lead, certification bodies, regulators, fleet operators, and potentially courts. They need to understand the implications of your research without reading the full technical document.

**Requirements for human-stakeholder communication:**
1. **Include a plain-language executive summary** at the top of every research document. Maximum 200 words. No jargon. State what you found, why it matters, and what should happen next.
2. **Use the NEXUS Glossary** (`[[nexus_glossary.md]]` — 310 terms) as the definitive terminology reference. If a term is not in the glossary, define it in your document and note that it should be added to the glossary.
3. **Quantify risk.** When your research identifies a risk, quantify it: "This vulnerability could allow an adversarial agent to deploy unsafe bytecode with estimated probability X per attempt, resulting in an estimated PFH increase from 10⁻⁷/h to 10⁻⁵/h, which exceeds SIL 1 requirements."
4. **Distinguish engineering recommendations from research findings.** "We proved X" is a research finding. "Based on this finding, we recommend Y" is an engineering recommendation. Human stakeholders need to know the difference, especially when making certification decisions.

## 6. Quality Gates

### 6.1 Complete Analysis

A research analysis is **complete** when it meets all of the following criteria:

1. **Research question stated:** The document begins with a clearly formulated research question (Section 1.3 format).
2. **Prior work acknowledged:** All directly relevant prior findings are cited and their relationship to this analysis is stated.
3. **Evidence meets the standard:** Every claim is supported by evidence at or above the minimum level for its claim type (Section 3.2).
4. **Assumptions stated:** Every assumption underlying the analysis is explicitly listed.
5. **Limitations stated:** The boundaries of the analysis are clearly stated — what it proves/shows and what it does not.
6. **Findings are reproducible:** Another agent could reproduce the analysis from the information provided (proofs can be verified, simulations can be re-run, arguments can be followed).
7. **Open questions identified:** At least one specific open question is stated for future investigation.
8. **Cross-referenced:** All claims that depend on other documents are cross-referenced with specific document, section, and (where applicable) finding identifiers.
9. **Future work specified:** At least one concrete next step is proposed with enough detail for a future agent to begin work.
10. **Executive summary provided:** A plain-language summary is included for non-technical stakeholders.

**An analysis that meets fewer than 8 of these criteria is a draft, not a deliverable.**

### 6.2 Well-Formed Hypothesis

A hypothesis is **well-formed** when:
1. It is falsifiable: there exists a conceivable observation that would disprove it.
2. It is specific: it makes a precise prediction, not a vague claim.
3. It is grounded: it follows from established theory or empirical observation.
4. It is testable: the evidence required to evaluate it is obtainable (by proof, simulation, or measurement).
5. It has a scope: it applies to a specific context (a specific NEXUS component, domain, or scenario).

**Good hypothesis:** "H-TRUST-003: Under adversarial event flooding where the bad event rate switches between 2% and 15% with a 48-hour period, the trust score will oscillate with amplitude >0.1 around the equilibrium point, preventing stable autonomy level promotion." — Falsifiable (simulation can disprove it), specific (precise parameters and predicted amplitude), grounded (follows from switched dynamical systems theory), testable (simulation), scoped (trust score under specific adversarial conditions).

**Bad hypothesis:** "Agent-generated code might be less safe." — Not falsifiable (what would disprove "might"?), not specific (less safe by what measure?), not testable (no concrete prediction).

### 6.3 Valid Argument

An argument is **valid** when:
1. The conclusion follows logically from the premises.
2. Every premise is either (a) established by prior work (cited), (b) supported by evidence at the appropriate level, or (c) explicitly stated as an assumption.
3. No logical fallacies are present (affirming the consequent, false dichotomy, appeal to authority without evidence, etc.).
4. The argument does not overreach: the conclusion is no stronger than the premises support.
5. Counterarguments are considered and addressed.

### 6.4 Useful Open Problem

An open problem is **useful** when it meets these criteria:
1. **It blocks progress:** Solving the problem would unblock a specification, a certification pathway, a safety claim, or a design decision.
2. **It is specific:** A competent researcher can understand exactly what is being asked and what a solution would look like.
3. **It has success criteria:** The problem statement includes measurable criteria that distinguish a solution from a non-solution.
4. **It has dependencies identified:** The problem statement lists which other problems must be solved first (or can be solved in parallel).
5. **It has an estimated difficulty:** The problem statement includes an effort estimate and difficulty rating.
6. **It is grounded in the NEXUS architecture:** The problem references specific NEXUS components, specifications, or constraints.

**Reference:** The existing `open_problems_catalog.md` provides 29 exemplars of well-formed open problems.

### 6.5 Successful Synthesis

A synthesis is **successful** when:
1. It integrates at least 3 separate findings or research threads.
2. It identifies at least one insight that none of the contributing findings contained individually.
3. It resolves (or at least explains) any contradictions among the contributing findings.
4. It identifies gaps that the synthesis reveals.
5. It produces a coherent framework that future agents can use as a foundation.
6. It proposes concrete next steps that follow naturally from the synthesis.

## 7. Iteration Handoff Protocol

This is the most important section in this document. The quality of every future iteration depends on how well you hand off your work.

### 7.1 What Goes Into the Handoff

At the end of your iteration, you must produce a **handoff package** consisting of:

#### A. Work Record (appended to `worklog.md`)

Follow the format established by previous agents in the worklog. Include:
- Task ID and agent designation
- What you set out to do
- What documents you read (list all of them)
- What you produced (list all deliverables with file paths and word counts)
- Key findings (list finding identifiers with one-line summaries)
- Open questions you could not resolve
- Specific recommendations for the next iteration

#### B. Document Status Update

For every document you created or modified, update the `Status` field in the header:
- `Draft` — Work in progress, not yet reviewed
- `Under Review` — Complete, awaiting validation
- `Accepted` — Reviewed and accepted by at least one other agent or human
- `Superseded by [document]` — This document has been replaced
- `Deprecated` — This document's conclusions are no longer valid due to system changes

#### C. Research Progress Map

Create or update a brief progress map showing the current state of every open problem you worked on:

```markdown
## Research Progress Map (Iteration N)

| Problem | Status | What Was Done | What Remains | Next Agent Should |
|---------|--------|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| P-001 (Certification Paradox) | ADVANCED | Drafted PCCP outline; identified 3 evidence types needed | Engage Lloyd's Register; formalize process model | Begin regulatory engagement; complete evidence artifacts |
| P-002 (Agent Type System) | IN PROGRESS | Proved interval arithmetic decidable (F-TYPE-001); need implementation | Implement interval analysis on Jetson; benchmark against 500 reflexes | Implement and benchmark; extend to PID state |

```

#### D. Finding Registry Update

If you produced new findings (F-), hypotheses (H-), or contradictions (C-), add them to a consolidated finding registry section at the end of your handoff. Format:

```markdown
|-----------|------|-----------|---------------|----------|--------|
```

### 7.2 Documenting Open Threads

An **open thread** is a line of investigation that you started but could not complete. Open threads are the most valuable part of your handoff — they tell the next agent exactly where to start.

For each open thread, provide:

```markdown
### Open Thread: [Descriptive title]

**Started in:** [document and section]
**What was established:** [What you figured out before hitting a wall]
**What blocked progress:** [Specific reason you could not continue]
**What the next agent should do:** [Specific next step with enough detail to begin]
**Estimated effort to complete:** [Time estimate]
**Dependencies:** [What else needs to happen first]
```

**Good open thread:**
> ### Open Thread: PID Controller State in Abstract Interpretation
>
> **Started in:** `agent-type-system_analysis.md` Section 5.3
> **What was established:** Interval arithmetic handles all 32 opcodes except `PID_COMPUTE`, which is a syscall that updates internal PID controller state. The PID controller implements the discrete transfer function `u[n] = Kp*e[n] + Ki*sum(e[0..n])*dt + Kd*(e[n]-e[n-1])/dt`, where `e[n]` is the error signal. If we model the PID controller as part of the abstract interpretation, the interval state must include the integral accumulator `sum(e[0..n])`, which can grow unboundedly.
> **What blocked progress:** I was unable to derive a tight bound on the integral accumulator that is both sound (never underestimates the true accumulator) and precise enough to produce useful actuator bounds. The naive bound (assuming maximum error for all time) is too wide — it predicts the actuator output could be ±infinity.
> **What the next agent should do:** Research existing abstract interpretation techniques for IIR filters and PID controllers. Key references: Cousot & Cousot (1977) on widening operators for loops; Feret (2004) on interprocedural abstract interpretation with accumulators. The specific challenge is finding a widening operator that converges to a tight bound on the PID integral term within reasonable time. If this is infeasible, an alternative is to require the agent to declare the PID parameters as part of the bytecode metadata and use parameter-specific transfer function analysis.
> **Estimated effort to complete:** 9-12 person-months (if a novel widening operator is needed) or 3-6 months (if existing techniques can be adapted).
> **Dependencies:** None — this can proceed independently.

### 7.3 Marking Partial Work

### 7.5 Anti-Patterns: What Not to Do in a Handoff

**Anti-pattern 1: The Silent Abandonment.** You start investigating a problem, hit a wall, and simply don't mention it in your handoff. The next agent wastes hours re-discovering the same wall. *Correction:* Document every open thread, even if the progress was "I tried approach X and it failed because Y."

**Anti-pattern 2: The Overconfident Draft.** You submit a document with `Status: Accepted` when it has not been reviewed by another agent or human. *Correction:* Use `Draft` or `Under Review` until another agent has validated your work.

**Anti-pattern 3: The Orphaned Reference.** Your document references `[[some_document.md]]` but that document does not exist. *Correction:* Verify every cross-reference before submitting. If you need a document that does not exist, note it as a gap in your handoff.

**Anti-pattern 4: The Vague Handoff.** You write "The next agent should continue the work on trust dynamics." *Correction:* "The next agent should complete the Lyapunov stability analysis started in Section 4 of trust-equilibrium_analysis.md. The specific task is to prove that V(T) = -d(T)/dt (the rate of trust decrease) is a Lyapunov function for all admissible switching sequences defined in Section 3.2. If this proof fails, the fallback is to identify bounded switching sequences that guarantee convergence (see the partial proof in Section 4.3, which handles the case where the bad event rate switches between exactly two values)."

**Anti-pattern 5: The Missing Context.** Your document uses NEXUS-specific terminology without defining it, assuming the reader has read `claude.md`. *Correction:* Define terms on first use or link to the glossary. Every document must be self-contained.
